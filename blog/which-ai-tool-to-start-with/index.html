ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini or Copilot: which should you actually start with?

Naomi Shiels·13 June 2026·6 min read

If you have spent any time reading about AI, you have probably hit the same wall most people do. Not “how do I use it”, but “which one do I even pick”. ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot. Four names, four logos, a hundred opinions, and a quiet fear that if you choose wrong you will waste your time or look foolish.

Here is the honest truth, and I will say it up front so you can relax. For a beginner, any of the four is fine. The skills you build on one carry straight over to the others. So the choice matters far less than people fear.

I am Naomi. I am not an engineer. I learned these tools the way you will, by showing up and trying things, and I now use them every day to write and to run a small business. I have used all four of these. Let me save you the agonising.

Quick answer

Start with the tool that matches the world you already live in.

  • Live in Gmail, Google Docs, an Android phone? Start with Gemini (it is Google’s).
  • Live in Outlook, Windows, Word and Excel? Start with Copilot (it is Microsoft’s).
  • No strong tie either way? Pick ChatGPT or Claude. Honestly, flip a coin.

All four have a free version that is more than enough to learn on. You can switch later, and your skills come with you. That is the whole answer. The rest of this is just the reasoning, in case you want it.

Why “match your existing world” is the real rule

People expect the right answer to be about which AI is cleverest. It almost never is, not at the beginner stage. The thing that actually decides whether you keep using a tool is friction. The small daily annoyances that make you quietly give up.

Matching your existing world removes that friction. If your email, your documents and your calendar already live with Google, then Gemini is sitting right there inside them. It can see the email you are looking at and help you reply, without you copying anything across. Same story with Copilot if your life runs on Microsoft. It lives inside Word and Outlook, where you already are.

That is the difference between a tool you reach for ten times a day and one you forget you signed up for. Not raw intelligence. Convenience. Choose the one that meets you where you already are, and you will actually use it.

A fair, honest line on each

The differences between these four are real, but they are differences of accent, not of language. They all do the same core things. Here is how I would describe each one to a friend.

ChatGPT (from OpenAI). The best known of the four, which matters more than it sounds. It has a genuinely capable free version, and because so many people use it, there is more help online than for any of the others. If you get stuck, a quick search usually finds someone who has had your exact problem. A safe, well-trodden first step.

Claude (from Anthropic). This is the one I reach for most when words matter. It tends to write in a natural, careful way, with fewer of those stiff “as an AI” phrases. I use it for letters, for getting family history down on paper, and for thinking a problem through out loud when I am not sure what I actually mean yet. It has a solid free version too.

Gemini (from Google). Google’s own tool. Its real strength is where it lives, right alongside Gmail, Google Docs and your Android phone. If that is your world, Gemini is the path of least resistance, and it is good at the everyday jobs most people actually need.

Copilot (from Microsoft). Microsoft’s tool, and the mirror image of Gemini. It sits inside Windows, Word, Excel and Outlook. If you spend your working day in those, Copilot is right there in the toolbar, ready to draft, summarise or tidy without you leaving the document.

Notice what I am not doing. I am not telling you one is clearly best. At the level you are starting from, they are far closer than the marketing suggests. The accent differs. The job they do does not.

You do not need to pay

I want to be clear about this, because it stops a lot of people before they begin. Every one of these four has a free version, and the free version is genuinely enough to learn on. You can spend weeks getting comfortable, writing real things, getting real help, without putting in a card.

Paid plans exist, and they add speed, higher limits and a few extra features. But that is a decision for later, once you actually know whether you want more. Not a gate you have to pass through to start. Start free. Always.

And while we are on cards: never put your passwords, bank details, card numbers or ID into any of these tools. You are the Captain here. The AI is your crew. It is brilliant at drafting and shaping, but it does not need your secrets to help you, and you should never give them.

You can switch later, and your skills come with you

This is the bit that should take the pressure off completely. The skill you are really learning is not “how to use ChatGPT”. It is how to ask for what you want clearly, how to give the tool enough context, how to push back when the answer is not quite right, and how to check its facts before you trust them.

That skill is the same on all four. I move between them constantly depending on the job, and switching feels like moving between two cars. Different dashboard, same driving. So pick one, get good at asking, and know the door is open if you ever want to change.

A 2-minute test you can run yourself

If you genuinely cannot decide, here is the only test worth doing. It takes about two minutes and tells you more than any review.

  1. Open two of them in two browser tabs (say ChatGPT in one, Claude in the other). Both free, no payment needed.
  2. Give them the exact same request. Use something real from your own life. For example: “Write a warm, short message to my neighbour asking if we can move our shared bin night to Thursdays.”
  3. Read both answers side by side.
  4. Ask yourself one question. Which one sounds more like something I would actually send?

That is it. Whichever voice you prefer, start there. You are not grading them on cleverness. You are noticing which one feels like a better fit for you, and there is no wrong answer.

A word of honesty while you do this. All of these tools can sound confident while being wrong, especially about facts, dates, names and numbers. That is not a reason to avoid them. It is a reason to treat them as a clever crew member whose work you always check, not an oracle. Shape with it, then verify.

FAQ

Is ChatGPT the best one? It is the best known, and that brings real benefits: a strong free version and the most help available online when you get stuck. “Best known” and “best for you” are not the same thing, though. For a beginner, the gap between the four is small.

Do I have to pay to use AI? No. All four have a free version that is plenty for learning and for everyday use. Paid plans add speed and higher limits, but that is a later decision, not a starting requirement.

What if I pick one and then regret it? You will be fine. The skills transfer completely. Switching tools is quick, and everything you have learned about asking well comes with you. There is no lock-in to fear.
