# Microsoft Copilot: AI Built Into Windows and Office

**Course summary:** A five-lesson mini-course on Microsoft Copilot for Australian adults aged 50+ who use Windows computers and Microsoft Office. Covers the free personal Copilot, Edge browser integration, Word and Outlook features, and practical everyday use. Suitable for NotebookLM, video, and podcast format.

## Who This Course Is For

This course is for anyone who uses a Windows computer or any Microsoft product — Word, Outlook, Excel, or PowerPoint. If you have been using these tools for years, Copilot is the AI that works inside them.

This course is especially useful if the idea of signing up to a new service, creating a new account, or learning a new app feels too much. Copilot is already on your Windows computer. You may not have found it yet.

## Lesson 1: What Is Microsoft Copilot and Why Should You Care?

### Running time: approximately 10 minutes

Microsoft Copilot is AI built into the software you probably already use. If you have Windows 11, Copilot is in your computer right now, sitting in the taskbar, waiting.

The name "Copilot" is deliberate — Microsoft uses it to mean a co-worker who sits alongside you, helps when needed, and stays out of the way when not needed. It does not take over. It assists.

Here is the most honest description of what makes Copilot different from ChatGPT or Claude: it is in Microsoft Office.

For anyone who writes in Word — letters, documents, family histories, community newsletters — Copilot is a button in the Word toolbar. Highlight text and ask it to improve it. Describe what you want to write and ask it to draft a first version. Ask it to summarise a long document in a few sentences.

For anyone who uses Outlook for email — Copilot in Outlook summarises long email threads with one click. It drafts replies. If you receive a complex email and are not sure how to respond, you can ask Copilot to draft something and then edit it.

**The advantage for Microsoft users.** You do not need to learn a new place to work. You do not need to copy and paste things between apps. The AI is in the tools you already have.

**Who made it.** Microsoft, the company behind Windows, Office, and Xbox, invested billions of dollars in OpenAI — the company that makes ChatGPT. Copilot runs on OpenAI's GPT-4o models. In many ways, Copilot and ChatGPT are the same AI in different homes.

## Lesson 2: Setting It Up — Step by Step

### Running time: approximately 8 minutes

There are several ways to access Copilot. Start with the easiest one — you may not need to do anything at all.

### Finding Copilot in Windows 11

1. Look at your taskbar (the bar at the bottom of your screen). You may see a Copilot icon — it looks like a small colourful circle or sparkle.
2. Click it. A Copilot panel opens on the right side of your screen.
3. If you do not see this icon, click the Start button (Windows logo, bottom left) and search for "Copilot."
4. Alternatively, press the keyboard shortcut: the Windows key + C.

That is it. No installation, no new account required.

### Using Copilot via the Web (any computer, any browser)

1. Open any browser and go to **https://copilot.microsoft.com**
2. You can use Copilot without signing in, but signing in with a Microsoft account saves your conversation history.
3. If you have a Hotmail, Outlook.com, or MSN email address, you already have a Microsoft account. Use those credentials.

### Using Copilot in Microsoft Edge

Microsoft Edge is the browser that comes pre-installed with Windows. Using Edge adds Copilot to your browsing experience.

1. Open Microsoft Edge (it is in your taskbar or Start menu — the blue wave icon)
2. Look for the Copilot icon in the top-right corner of Edge's toolbar. It looks like a small circle with a sparkle.
3. Click it. A Copilot panel opens alongside whatever web page you are viewing.
4. You can now ask questions about the page you are reading: "Summarise this article" or "What is the main argument being made here?"

### Using Copilot in Word (requires Copilot Pro or Microsoft 365)

1. Open Microsoft Word
2. Look for a small Copilot icon in the toolbar (it may be labelled "Copilot" with a sparkle icon)
3. Click it to open the Copilot panel
4. Type what you want: "Draft a cover letter for a volunteer coordinator role" and read the result
5. You can also highlight existing text, right-click, and choose "Rewrite with Copilot"

## Lesson 3: Your First Five Conversations

### Running time: approximately 15 minutes

---

### Conversation 1: Asking a question while reading the news

Open Microsoft Edge and go to any news website — ABC News (abc.net.au), The Guardian, or wherever you normally read news. Find an article about a topic you are following. Open the Copilot panel on the right.

> **"Can you summarise this article for me in three bullet points? And tell me — is there important context I should know about this topic that the article doesn't include?"**

This is the Edge integration working at its best: you see the article on the left; the summary and context appear on the right. You get more from the article without having to read every word.

---

### Conversation 2: Drafting a letter in Word

Open a new blank Word document. Open the Copilot panel. Type:

> **"Please draft a formal letter to the council requesting that the footpath outside my property be repaired. The footpath has a significant crack and raised section that is a trip hazard. I've called once but nothing has been done in three months. I'd like the letter to be polite but firm, and I want a response within 21 days. I live in [your suburb]."**

Copilot produces a draft that you can then edit, expand, or customise. Click "Insert" to place the draft into your Word document.

---

### Conversation 3: Summarising an email thread in Outlook

If you use Outlook (the desktop app that comes with Microsoft Office), look for the Copilot icon when you open a long email thread.

Click the icon or button that says "Summarise." Copilot reads the entire thread and gives you a paragraph summary of what has been discussed and any action items.

If you need to reply:
> **"Draft a response to this email thread. My key points: I agree to the proposed time, I'll need to reschedule if it rains, and I'd like a reminder two days before. Tone: friendly."**

---

### Conversation 4: Generating an image for a card or post

In Copilot (any interface), try the image generation feature:

> **"Can you create an image of an Australian garden in spring, with a wooden garden bench, roses in bloom, a butterfly, and warm afternoon light? I'd like a soft, watercolour illustration style."**

Copilot will generate the image. Right-click to save it, then use it in a card, email, or personal project.

The image generation in Copilot is powered by DALL-E 3 (the same technology as ChatGPT's image generation) and produces high-quality results.

---

### Conversation 5: Getting help with Windows

Copilot in Windows 11 can help you understand and use your own computer. This is a unique feature.

> **"My printer stopped working after the last Windows update. What should I try to fix it?"**

Or:

> **"I want to back up all my photos from my computer to an external hard drive. Can you walk me through how to do that in Windows 11?"**

Copilot can give you step-by-step instructions for Windows tasks, see your current settings, and provide guidance relevant to your specific computer setup.

## Lesson 4: The Things Nobody Tells You

### Running time: approximately 10 minutes

**The different Copilots are confusing.** "Copilot" appears in Windows, in Edge, in Word, in Excel, in Outlook, in Teams, and as a standalone website. Each one has slightly different features and capabilities. The free personal Copilot (in Windows and at copilot.microsoft.com) is different from the paid Microsoft 365 Copilot (in Word and Outlook). The confusion is real and acknowledged by Microsoft.

**The "Recall" feature — know what it is.** Microsoft introduced a feature called Recall that takes regular screenshots of everything you do on your PC, stores them, and makes them searchable by AI. Privacy advocates raised significant concerns when this feature was announced. It is off by default in most configurations but may be on in some. Check: Settings > Privacy and security > Recall and snapshots. Many security experts recommend disabling it.

**Free Copilot limitations.** The free Copilot in Windows has a daily limit and some capability restrictions. For the full Office integration (Word, Outlook, Excel) you need Copilot Pro (approximately AUD $30-36/month). Starting with the free version is sensible.

**Writing quality.** Copilot and ChatGPT use similar underlying models and produce similar writing quality. For drafting functional documents — emails, letters, forms — Copilot is excellent. For writing that requires real craft — eulogies, speeches, creative writing — Claude tends to produce more natural-sounding prose.

**Microsoft and privacy.** Microsoft's commercial data protection policy means that Microsoft 365 Copilot subscriptions do not use your data to train AI models. This is a specific and explicit commitment. For the free personal Copilot, standard Microsoft privacy terms apply. Check your privacy settings at account.microsoft.com.

**Edge vs your current browser.** Using Copilot in Edge is one of the best ways to access Copilot's browsing features, but it requires switching from Chrome or Safari to Edge. If you prefer your current browser, you can still use Copilot at copilot.microsoft.com — just without the page-reading integration.

## Lesson 5: Making It Part of Your Life

### Running time: approximately 8 minutes

For Windows and Microsoft Office users, Copilot integration into existing tools is its defining advantage.

**Make Edge your default browser for a week.** If you use Microsoft products, give Edge a week as your default browser. With Copilot in the sidebar, you get an AI assistant alongside every webpage you visit. Many users who switch describe it as the moment AI "clicked" for them — not as a separate app you go to, but as a sidebar that is always there.

**Start using the Word Copilot for first drafts.** Instead of starting with a blank page, open Copilot and describe what you want to write. The draft it gives you is a starting point — always edit, always make it yours. But having something to react to is much easier than creating from nothing.

**Use Outlook summarise for inbox triage.** When you open Outlook after a busy few days, use the email summarise feature before anything else. Get the lay of the land in two minutes, then respond to what actually matters.

**Image generation for personal projects.** The image generation feature in the free tier is underused. If you send cards, create social posts, or contribute to community newsletters, experiment with generating images through Copilot.

**The tech support habit.** Whenever you encounter something confusing about your Windows computer — a setting you cannot find, a feature that is not working, an update that changed something — ask Copilot in Windows first. It is often faster and more accurate than searching online.
