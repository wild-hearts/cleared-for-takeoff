## Lesson 1: What Is Grammarly and Why Should You Care?

### Running time: approximately 10 minutes

Writing is something most of us do every day. An email to a doctor's surgery. A message to a grandchild. A response in a Facebook group. A complaint to a company. A letter to a council.

For most of these, we write, re-read once or twice, and send. And sometimes we later spot a mistake — a word we missed, an autocorrect that changed what we meant, a sentence that reads more curt than we intended. And sometimes the person on the other end misreads our tone — we meant friendly, they read formal; we meant firm, they read aggressive.

Grammarly watches for all of this.

It sits invisibly in your browser — in Gmail, in Facebook, in any text box on any website — and quietly underlines issues as you type. A red underline for a clear error. A blue underline for a suggestion. Hover over the underline to see the suggestion and explanation. Click to accept, or ignore it if it is wrong for your purpose.

The AI element matters because Grammarly's suggestions have become significantly more sophisticated over time. It is no longer just a grammar checker — it detects tone, flags clarity issues, suggests better word choices, and (in its AI-powered features) can rewrite and generate text for you.

**Why it matters specifically for this audience:**

Many people aged 50+ write a significant amount of important correspondence. Disputes with service providers. Letters to government departments. Emails to medical professionals. Formal requests. These are communications where getting the tone and clarity right genuinely matters — where a single misread sentence can create confusion or conflict.

Grammarly does not write these for you. But it helps you write them better.

**The company.** Grammarly was founded in 2009 by three people, two of whom are Ukrainian. Despite the Silicon Valley address, Grammarly has a significant engineering team in Kyiv. The company has approximately 40 million daily users worldwide.

## Lesson 2: Setting It Up — Step by Step

### Running time: approximately 8 minutes

The best way to use Grammarly is through the browser extension, which means it works automatically everywhere you write online.

### Installing the Browser Extension (Chrome or Edge)

1. Go to **https://www.grammarly.com**
2. Click "Get Grammarly for free"
3. You will be prompted to create an account. Sign up with your Google account, Apple account, or email address.
4. After signing up, Grammarly takes you through setup. When prompted, click "Add to Chrome" (if using Chrome) or "Add to Microsoft Edge" (if using Edge).
5. A small pop-up appears in your browser. Click "Add extension."
6. The Grammarly icon appears in your browser toolbar — a small green "G."

That is it. Grammarly is now active in your browser.

### Testing It

1. Go to your Gmail (mail.google.com) or any website with a text box
2. Click in any text box
3. Start typing something — any sentence
4. If you make an error, you will see an underline appear. Hover over it to see the suggestion.

### For Microsoft Word

1. Go to https://www.grammarly.com/office-addin/mac-and-windows
2. Download the Grammarly for Microsoft Office installer
3. Run the installer (double-click the downloaded file)
4. Open Word — a Grammarly panel appears in the sidebar

### Creating and Signing Into Your Account

When you installed the extension, you created an account. Your Grammarly account is where:
- Your usage statistics and writing history are stored
- You access your full document editor at app.grammarly.com
- You can upgrade to Pro if you choose

Sign in at grammarly.com whenever you want to access your documents or check your settings.

### Free vs Pro: What Matters

**Free:** Grammar and spell checking, basic tone detection, 100 AI prompts per month

**Pro (~AUD $18/month billed annually):** Advanced style suggestions, full tone adjustment, unlimited AI prompts, plagiarism detection, full document editor features

Start free. If you write often and find yourself wanting more AI capabilities, Pro is well priced at the annual rate.

## Lesson 3: Your First Five Writing Sessions

### Running time: approximately 15 minutes

---

### Session 1: Let Grammarly check an email before you send it

Open Gmail. Start writing an email — any real email you need to write today.

As you type, watch for underlines appearing. When you see one:
- Hover over it — a small pop-up shows the suggestion and reason
- Click the suggestion to accept it
- Or click "Dismiss" if it is not relevant to your meaning

When you have finished writing, look at the Grammarly card that often appears in the corner of Gmail. It shows your text's overall score and a summary of issues found. Click it for more detail.

This is Grammarly at its most basic and useful — the safety net under your everyday email.

---

### Session 2: Check the tone of a message

Write an email or message where tone matters — a complaint, a request, something to a professional, something to a difficult family member.

After writing it, look for the Grammarly tone indicator. It will tell you whether your message reads as formal, informal, confident, hesitant, friendly, direct — or a combination.

Ask: is this how I want to come across? If not, ask Grammarly to adjust it.

In the Grammarly editor (app.grammarly.com) or in the Gmail Grammarly panel:
- Find the "Ask AI" or sparkle button
- Type: "Make this email sound warmer and less formal, while keeping the main request clear"

Compare the result with your original and decide which works better.

---

### Session 3: Use AI to draft a difficult letter

Sometimes the hardest part of writing is starting. Use Grammarly's AI to give you a starting point.

In app.grammarly.com, create a new document. In the toolbar, click the AI button (sparkle icon) and type:

> **"Draft a formal letter to my health insurer disputing a claim they denied. The claim was for physiotherapy following a GP referral for lower back pain. I've been a member for 12 years. I want to request a review and ask for a written explanation of the specific policy provision that led to the denial. Tone: formal but not aggressive."**

Grammarly produces a draft. Read it, edit it to add your specific details and personal voice, and use it as the basis for your final letter.

---

### Session 4: Write a better Facebook post

Open Facebook on your computer (not the phone app) and click in the "What's on your mind?" box.

Start writing a post — something you want to share with friends or a community group.

Watch Grammarly activate in the text box. The suggestions it makes will be more about clarity and word choice than strict grammar, since Facebook posts have a different standard than formal letters.

After writing the post, try asking Grammarly AI to:
> "Make this post more engaging and add a question at the end to encourage comments"

Then compare the two versions and decide what feels most like you.

---

### Session 5: Improve something you have already written

Find an email, letter, or document you wrote recently that you were not entirely happy with. Paste it into app.grammarly.com.

Look at what Grammarly identifies. Then try this specific request in the AI panel:

> "Can you review this and tell me: (1) what is working well, (2) what is unclear or could be expressed better, and (3) one specific change that would most improve it?"

This kind of feedback — specific and constructive — is something a good editor would provide, and it genuinely improves writing over time.

## Lesson 4: The Things Nobody Tells You

### Running time: approximately 10 minutes

**Grammarly reads everything you type.** This is its entire point — it cannot help you without seeing what you write. This means that when the extension is active, your emails, messages, and posts pass through Grammarly's systems as you type them.

For most everyday writing, this is an acceptable tradeoff. But for any text box where you are entering genuinely sensitive information — your bank's website, a medical portal, a government login — turn Grammarly off temporarily.

To turn off on a specific site: right-click the Grammarly icon in your toolbar and select "Turn off Grammarly on this site." You can turn it back on the same way.

**Its suggestions are not always right.** Grammarly may flag intentional choices: Australian English ("recognise" flagged as an error because it defaults to American English), informal phrases, deliberate fragments for effect, or technical terms it doesn't recognise. Click "Dismiss" when the suggestion is wrong for your purpose. You are always in control.

**Set it to Australian English.** By default, Grammarly may check against American English. To change this:
1. Go to grammarly.com and sign in
2. Click your account icon (top right) > Settings > Language
3. Select "English (Australian)" or "English (British)" if Australian is not listed

This prevents it from flagging correctly spelled Australian words as errors.

**Over-relying on it is a risk.** Using Grammarly as your final check is excellent. Using it as your only thinking about the quality of your writing is a risk — it catches errors and suggests improvements, but it cannot tell you whether your argument is sound, whether you have said everything you meant, or whether the letter will actually achieve what you want.

**The "suggestions accepted" habit.** Grammarly tracks which suggestions you accept and which you decline. Over time, this personalises its suggestions to your writing style. It gets better the more you use it.

## Lesson 5: Making It Part of Your Life

### Running time: approximately 8 minutes

Grammarly is a background tool — it does its best work when you forget it is there and find its suggestions waiting for you.

**The single most important setup step:** Once you have installed the extension and set it to Australian English, the daily habit is automatic. It is always on, always checking. You do not need to remember to use it.

**Build the habit of reading suggestions.** The discipline is not in using Grammarly — it is in reading what it finds before you dismiss it. The easy temptation is to ignore the underlines and click Send anyway. The habit of pausing for 30 seconds to review what Grammarly has caught is where the value lives.

**Use the AI feature for important correspondence.** Not for every email — for the ones that matter. When you are writing to a government department, a professional, or in a formal dispute, take the extra minute to run the letter through the AI with a tone and clarity check.

**The learning over time.** One of Grammarly's most underrated benefits: people who use it for a year report that they make fewer errors because they have learned from the feedback. Grammarly does not just fix your writing — it teaches you how to write better.

## Quick Reference Card

### Grammarly Underlines: What They Mean

- **Red underline:** Spelling or grammar error — always worth fixing
- **Blue underline:** Style, clarity, or tone suggestion — use your judgement
- **Green underline:** Word choice or vocabulary suggestion — optional

### AI Request Formulas

**For drafting:**
> "Draft a [document type] about [topic]. Key points: [list]. Tone: [tone]. Length: approximately [word count]."

**For tone adjustment:**
> "Rewrite this to sound more [warm/formal/confident/friendly], while keeping the main message the same."

**For editing feedback:**
> "Review this writing. Tell me what is working and what could be improved. Then give me a revised version."

**For clarity:**
> "Is my main point clear in this message? What could I change to make it clearer?"
