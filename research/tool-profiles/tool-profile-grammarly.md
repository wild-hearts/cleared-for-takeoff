# Tool Profile: Grammarly AI

**Document summary:** A comprehensive profile of Grammarly for Australian adults aged 50+. Grammarly is a writing assistant that has evolved from grammar checking into a full AI writing partner. Covers what it is, pricing, strengths, limitations, privacy, and five practical prompts.

**Last updated:** May 2026

## What Is Grammarly and Who Makes It?

Grammarly is a writing assistance tool founded in 2009 by two Ukrainians, Max Lytvyn and Alex Shevchenko, and a Canadian, Brad Hoover. The company is headquartered in San Francisco with a significant engineering presence in Kyiv.

Grammarly started as a grammar and spell-checker. Over 15 years, it has evolved into a full AI writing assistant that can edit for style, tone, and clarity, generate text from scratch, and adapt your writing to different audiences and purposes.

As of 2026, Grammarly has approximately 40 million daily users. It runs as a browser extension (meaning it appears in whatever website or app you are writing in — Gmail, Facebook, any text box), as a desktop application, and as a standalone editor at app.grammarly.com.

The key practical point: if you install the Grammarly browser extension, it will gently assist you every time you type anything in Chrome or Edge — an email, a Facebook comment, a text to your grandchildren. You do not have to go to a separate app. The help comes to you.

## What It Is Genuinely Good At

**Catching what you miss when you re-read your own work.** We all read what we meant to write, not what we actually wrote. Grammarly spots the errors your eye slides over: a missing word, a confused "there/their/they're," a run-on sentence that made sense when you wrote it but reads oddly to fresh eyes.

**Tone adjustment.** This is the feature that surprises people. Grammarly can tell you that your email sounds defensive, or formal when you wanted friendly, or condescending when you wanted helpful. You can then ask it to "rewrite this to sound warmer" or "make this more confident." For anyone who has ever worried about how a message will land, this is genuinely useful.

**Available everywhere.** The browser extension is Grammarly's superpower. It appears in Gmail, in Facebook comments, in whatever you are typing online. You do not have to copy and paste your text somewhere else to get help — the help appears where you are already working.

**Microsoft Word integration.** Grammarly installs a sidebar inside Microsoft Word. It checks your document as you type and offers suggestions in a panel on the right. For anyone who writes in Word, this is seamless.

**Clarity suggestions.** Grammarly often spots sentences that are grammatically correct but unclear — sentences that say what you meant, but not quite clearly enough. "Consider splitting this sentence" or "This could be read two ways" are the kinds of suggestions that make your writing cleaner.

**Explanation of suggestions.** Grammarly does not just underline errors — it explains why it is suggesting a change. "This subject and verb don't agree," or "Consider a different word for clarity here." Over time, you learn from its suggestions and make fewer errors.

**Inclusive language guidance.** Grammarly flags phrases that may be outdated, exclusive, or unintentionally offensive, with gentle suggestions. Useful for anyone writing for diverse audiences.

## What It Struggles With

**It cannot replicate your voice.** Grammarly's AI has a tendency to produce writing that sounds slightly generic — correct, clear, but not quite you. For formal documents, this is fine. For personal writing — a letter to a dear friend, a family history, a eulogy — the AI can sand the personality out of your prose. Be thoughtful about which suggestions you accept.

**Over-correction is a risk.** Grammarly will sometimes flag correct, intentional choices as errors — particularly if your writing is deliberately informal or uses Australian idioms. "No worries" may get flagged. "Reckon" might be questioned. You are always in control of whether to accept a suggestion.

**AI writing quality varies.** The generated text can be good for short, functional tasks (drafting an email) but less good for longer, nuanced pieces. For serious writing projects, the AI writing in Claude or ChatGPT is stronger.

**Privacy trade-off.** Grammarly reads everything you type when the extension is active. This includes emails, private messages, financial documents, and sensitive correspondence. This is inherent to how it works — it cannot help you without seeing what you are writing.

**Can be distracting.** If you are a writer who drafts freely and edits later, the constant underlining of errors while you type can interrupt your flow. You can disable suggestions while drafting and run Grammarly over the finished text.

## Best Use Cases for Someone Aged 50+

1. **Professional emails and formal correspondence.** Letters of complaint, insurance claims, applications, letters to government departments — any email where you want to sound confident and professional. Grammarly catches errors and the tone-check feature helps you strike the right note.

2. **Email in general.** For anyone who writes significant amounts of email and worries about typos or mistakes reaching grandchildren, friends, or professionals — having Grammarly quietly check every message is peace of mind for a low cost.

3. **Online forms and applications.** Grammarly works in web browsers, so it assists with online forms, website contact forms, community website posts, and more.

4. **Writing projects over time.** For anyone working on a memoir, a family history, a self-published book, or regular contributions to a community newsletter, the consistent style and clarity feedback over time is genuinely useful.

5. **Facebook and social media.** Grammarly runs in Facebook and many social media platforms via the browser extension. It means the comment you dashed off in a rush gets checked before you post.

6. **Learning better writing habits.** Because Grammarly explains its suggestions, using it over months genuinely improves your writing. Many users report that after a year with Grammarly, they make fewer errors because they have learned from the feedback.

## How to Get Started: Step by Step

**Option 1 — Browser extension (recommended for most users):**
1. Go to **https://www.grammarly.com**
2. Click "Get Grammarly for free"
3. Sign up with your email address or Google account
4. You will be prompted to add the Grammarly extension to your browser. Click "Add to Chrome" (or Edge, Firefox, Safari)
5. The extension icon appears in your browser toolbar (a small green G)
6. Now open Gmail, Facebook, or any website with a text box — Grammarly is active. Start typing.

**Option 2 — Standalone editor:**
1. Go to **https://app.grammarly.com**
2. Sign in to your account
3. Click "New Document"
4. Type or paste your text
5. Grammarly's suggestions appear in the right panel

**Option 3 — Microsoft Word:**
1. After creating your Grammarly account, go to https://www.grammarly.com/office-addin/mac-and-windows
2. Download and install the Grammarly for Microsoft Office add-in
3. Open Word — a Grammarly panel appears on the right side

**Understanding the suggestions:**
- Words with a red underline: spelling or grammar error
- Words with a blue underline: clarity or style suggestion
- Hover over the underline to see the suggestion and explanation
- Click the suggestion to accept it, or dismiss it if it's not right for your purpose

## Five Specific Prompts for This Audience

*Note: Grammarly is primarily reactive (it works on what you write) rather than prompt-driven. However, its AI features can generate text when you describe what you need. These examples use Grammarly's AI writing assistant, available through the sparkle/AI icon in the editor.*

**1. Drafting a formal complaint:**
> In Grammarly's AI panel: "Write a formal letter of complaint to an internet service provider. The problem is that my internet speed has been consistently below my contracted speed for three months. I have called twice with no resolution. I want to request compensation and a written response within 14 days. Tone: firm but professional."

**2. Writing a personal reference:**
> "Write a character reference letter for my neighbour who is applying for a volunteer position at a hospital. She is 55, retired nurse, organised, compassionate, and reliable. The letter is from me, a retired teacher, who has known her for 12 years."

**3. Rewriting for tone:**
> Paste any email you have drafted, then in the AI panel: "Rewrite this email so it sounds less formal and more warm — like I'm writing to a friend, not a colleague. Keep the key information but make it feel more personal."

**4. Checking a medical question:**
> Paste your draft letter to your GP or specialist, then: "Check this letter for clarity. I want to make sure my main concern is stated clearly in the first paragraph, and that I have not buried the most important question."

**5. Improving a social media post:**
> "Here is a post I want to put on my community Facebook group about our upcoming garden tour. Can you make it more engaging and add a call to action at the end?" [Paste your draft]

## Privacy and Data Considerations (Australian Law)

**Grammarly reads everything you type.** When the browser extension is active and you are in a text box, Grammarly is processing your text to make suggestions. This means Grammarly has access to your emails, messages, documents, and any other text you type in a browser.

**What Grammarly does with your data:** Grammarly states that it does not sell your text to third parties. However, Grammarly does use aggregated, anonymised data to improve its models. The company has explicit privacy commitments about what it does not do with your personal text.

**Disabling the extension on sensitive pages:** You can right-click the Grammarly icon and choose "Turn off Grammarly on this site" to disable it for specific websites — for example, your bank's website or an online medical form. This is worth doing for any website handling sensitive personal data.

**Grammarly's enterprise-grade privacy:** The company offers a Business tier with stronger privacy guarantees (no use of your text for training). For individual users, the standard privacy policy applies.

**Australian context:** Grammarly is not an Australian company and does not have localised Australian terms. Your data is stored on US-based servers. The OAIC guidance on AI applies: as an individual user, you are governed by Grammarly's own terms of service.

**Practical recommendation:** Install the extension for everyday email and social media writing. Consider disabling it on banking sites, medical portals, and any page where you are entering truly sensitive information.

## Honest Assessment: Is It Worth It?

The free plan delivers meaningful value for most users. Grammar and spell-checking, basic tone detection, and 100 AI prompts per month is sufficient for casual email and everyday writing.

The Pro plan at approximately AUD $18/month billed annually is worth it for anyone who writes professionally, frequently, or for an audience — community newsletters, formal correspondence, regular emails to professionals, or personal writing projects. The tone adjustment alone saves significant anxiety for people who worry about how their written communication is perceived.

The key insight: Grammarly works best when you think of it as a safety net and a learning tool, not as a replacement for your own judgement. Accept suggestions thoughtfully, not automatically.

**Verdict:** Excellent for everyday email and correspondence. Free plan is a solid starting point. Pro plan worth the annual subscription for regular writers. The convenience of having it work inside Gmail and Facebook is its greatest strength.
