## Where AI gives teachers planning time back

The honest case for AI in teaching is not about students. It is about the planning and admin that fills your evenings and weekends. Used well, AI is a teaching assistant for your own work. It does the first-draft heavy lifting, and you keep every piece of professional judgement. Here is what that looks like in practice.

Lesson and unit planning. You give it the year level, the curriculum outcomes, the time you have and the resources in the room, and it drafts a sequence of lessons or a unit overview. It will not know your class. You will. So you treat the draft as a starting point and shape it around the students in front of you.

Differentiating a resource. You have one worksheet and three reading levels in the room. Paste it in and ask for a simpler version, an extension version, and a scaffolded version with sentence starters. Ten minutes instead of an hour, and every student gets work that meets them where they are.

Rubrics and quick checks. Describe the task and the criteria, and it drafts a rubric or a set of quiz questions with answers. You read every line, because the wording of a criterion is a teaching decision, not a clerical one.

Report comments and parent communication. It helps you turn rough notes about a student into a clear, warm comment, or draft the email home about a behaviour concern or an excursion. You check that every sentence is true of that child before it goes anywhere.

Explaining a tricky concept three ways. Long division, photosynthesis, the causes of a war. Ask for three different explanations pitched at your year level, and you walk into the lesson with options for the student who did not get it the first way.

Plain summaries of dense documents. Drop in a curriculum document or a policy update and ask for a one-page plain-English summary so you can see what actually changed.

Two rules sit under all of it. First, this is your work, not the students'. AI plans your lessons; it does not do their learning. Second, check anything that matters, because it can state a wrong fact as confidently as a right one. When you use AI openly and verify it honestly, you also become the person who can teach students to do the same.

## Common questions about AI for educators

**Isn't AI just how students cheat?**
It can be, if no one shows a better way. This course is about your own planning and admin, not students' work. And understanding the tool honestly is exactly what lets you guide students to use it with integrity, rather than pretending it does not exist.

**Will it get facts wrong?**
Yes, sometimes, and confidently. So you check anything that matters before it reaches a student or a parent. That verifying habit is teachable, and modelling it openly is one of the most useful things you can do.

**How much time does it actually save?**
On the planning and admin, real hours most weeks. The drafting, differentiating and summarising are where the time comes back. The teaching judgement stays with you, which is the part that was never the bottleneck anyway.
