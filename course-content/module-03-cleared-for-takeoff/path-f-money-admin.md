# Module 3, Path F: Money and Admin

**Spoken duration:** ~14 minutes  
**Word count (spoken):** ~1,850 words  
**Format:** Direct to camera; on-screen prompt examples  
**On-screen text:** Prompt structures; live-source checklist  
**Tone:** Practical, matter-of-fact, reassuring  

---

[LOOK TO CAMERA]

Money and administration. For most people, this is the pile that never quite goes away.

[PAUSE]

The letter from the ATO that arrived two weeks ago and you are not sure how to interpret. The superannuation statement you have not opened because you are not sure what you would do with the information. The insurance renewal that seems more expensive than last year and you want to know if you should be doing something about it. The Centrelink form that you started and then put down because the language was confusing.

[PAUSE]

Most of this is not genuinely complicated. It feels complicated because it uses language you are not used to, asks for information in formats you are not familiar with, and carries just enough consequence that getting it wrong feels like a risk.

[PAUSE]

AI can help with all of it. Not by doing your taxes or managing your money — that would require your specific accounts and data — but by helping you understand what you are looking at, explaining the system, helping you prepare for conversations with professionals, and drafting correspondence.

[PAUSE]

This path covers five money and admin areas:

Understanding your financial statements.
Dealing with government agencies.
Insurance: reviewing, querying, and claiming.
Superannuation: understanding your position.
Budgeting and financial planning basics.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Area 1: Understanding Financial Statements"]

Bank statements, credit card statements, superannuation statements, tax assessments, investment summaries — these arrive in the mail or in the inbox and most people read them just enough to confirm nothing has gone catastrophically wrong, then file them.

[PAUSE]

AI can help you get significantly more out of these documents.

[ON SCREEN: Financial statement prompt]

"I have received a [type of statement]. I want to understand:

1. What this document is showing me.
2. What the key figures I should pay attention to are.
3. Whether anything looks unusual that I should query.
4. What questions this document prompts that I should ask my [bank / accountant / super fund / financial adviser].

Here is the relevant section: [paste, omitting any account numbers or identifying details]."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

The instruction to omit account numbers and identifying details is important and worth repeating. Paste the figures and the structure. Do not paste the account numbers, your date of birth, your Tax File Number, or any other identity information.

[PAUSE]

Understanding your documents regularly — not just when something goes wrong — gives you a much clearer picture of your financial position over time. AI makes that regular reading considerably easier.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Area 2: Dealing with Government Agencies"]

Centrelink, MyGov, the ATO, Medicare, Services Australia, aged care assessment teams, state revenue offices.

These agencies handle important things. They also communicate in formal language, operate through systems that are not always intuitive, and require documentation in specific formats.

[PAUSE]

AI can help in three ways here.

First: understanding what they are asking. If you have received correspondence from a government agency that is unclear, paste the relevant section and ask AI to explain it in plain English.

Second: understanding your options. "I have received a Centrelink debt notice. What are my options and what is the process for disputing it?" AI can explain the general process. The current specifics should be verified at servicesaustralia.gov.au.

Third: preparing correspondence. If you need to write to a government agency — to dispute a decision, request a review, provide information, or make a complaint — use AI to help draft the letter. Government agencies respond better to correspondence that is clear, factual, and organised.

[ON SCREEN: Government correspondence prompt]

"I need to write to [agency] about [situation]. I want to [dispute a decision / request a review / provide information / make a formal complaint].

The situation is: [brief description — two to three sentences].
I have [documentation / correspondence / reference numbers]: [list what you have].

Please draft a clear, professional letter of around 300 words. The tone should be factual and firm, not aggressive."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

For any formal government process — a review, an appeal, a complaint — keep copies of everything. AI can help you draft well, but the record-keeping is yours to manage.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Area 3: Insurance — Reviewing, Querying, and Claiming"]

Insurance is one of the areas where people pay significant amounts of money for cover they do not fully understand. When something goes wrong, they find out the hard way what is and is not covered.

[PAUSE]

AI can help you understand your policies better — before you need to claim.

[ON SCREEN: Insurance review prompt]

"I have [type of insurance — home and contents / car / health / life]. I want to understand:

1. What this policy typically covers and what it typically excludes.
2. What questions I should be asking my insurer to make sure I have appropriate cover.
3. What I should do to prepare if I need to make a claim.

Here is the relevant section of my policy: [paste the relevant section, removing any identifying details]."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

At renewal time, AI can also help you think through whether to stay with your current insurer or look at alternatives. "I am renewing my home and contents insurance. My current premium is $X for [level of cover]. What questions should I be asking to find out whether this is good value, and what alternatives should I be comparing?"

[PAUSE]

If you need to make a claim, AI can help you prepare: "I need to make an insurance claim for [event]. What documentation will I typically need? What should I say in my initial contact with the insurer? What are the common mistakes people make in the claims process?"

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Area 4: Superannuation — Understanding Your Position"]

Superannuation is the area where most Australians feel least informed — and where understanding your position can make a significant difference.

[PAUSE]

AI can explain superannuation concepts clearly:

How contributions are taxed.
How investment options work inside a super fund.
What transition to retirement means and how it works.
How the pension and super interact at retirement.
What it means to consolidate multiple funds.

[PAUSE]

For any specific question about your super, use AI to understand the concept, then contact your fund directly — or speak with a financial adviser — to get advice on your specific situation.

[ON SCREEN: Super understanding prompt]

"I want to understand [super concept] in plain language. I am [age, approximate] and [still working / approaching retirement / recently retired]. Can you explain how this works, what the key considerations are, and what I should be asking my super fund or a financial adviser?"

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

One specific use worth highlighting: comparing super funds. If you are thinking about consolidating super or moving to a different fund, AI can help you understand what to compare — fees, investment performance, insurance options, member services. It gives you the evaluation framework. The current fund-specific figures come from the funds themselves or from the ATO's YourSuper comparison tool.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Area 5: Budgeting and Financial Basics"]

This is the most personal category, and the one where AI works best when you are specific.

[PAUSE]

"Help me make a budget" is too broad. "I am on a fixed income of $X per fortnight. My fixed costs are approximately $Y. I want to understand where my money is going and where I might have flexibility" — that is a conversation AI can genuinely help with.

[ON SCREEN: Budget conversation prompt]

"I want to get a clearer picture of my finances. Here is my situation:

Income: [weekly or fortnightly figure]
Regular fixed costs: [list the main ones — rent or mortgage, utilities, rates, phone, insurance]
Variable costs I want to understand better: [list — groceries, transport, medical, other]

I want to:
[Build a basic budget / Find out where I have flexibility / Prepare for a specific upcoming expense / Understand whether my current spending is sustainable]

Please help me organise this and ask me any questions that would help you give me more useful guidance."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

Notice the last line of that prompt: "ask me any questions." This is the reverse-prompting technique from Module 2, applied to a financial conversation. AI will ask you things that help it understand your situation better — and that process of being asked good questions is often clarifying in itself.

[PAUSE]

Important boundaries for money and admin:

Do not paste your full bank account numbers, Tax File Number, superannuation account numbers, or passwords into any AI tool.
AI can explain the system and help you prepare. For specific personal advice on significant financial decisions, a qualified financial adviser is the right resource.
For current figures — tax thresholds, payment rates, super contribution caps — check the relevant official source.

[PAUSE]

ato.gov.au for tax information.
servicesaustralia.gov.au for Centrelink, Medicare, and payments.
moneysmart.gov.au for consumer-focused financial information.
Your super fund's own website for current fund-specific information.
