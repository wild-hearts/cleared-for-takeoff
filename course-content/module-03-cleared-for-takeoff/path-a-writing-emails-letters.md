# Module 3, Path A: Writing Emails and Letters

**Spoken duration:** ~14 minutes  
**Word count (spoken):** ~1,850 words  
**Format:** Direct to camera; on-screen examples of prompts and outputs  
**On-screen text:** Prompt examples; before/after comparisons  
**Tone:** Warm, practical, matter-of-fact  

---

[LOOK TO CAMERA]

Writing is one of the things most people find genuinely difficult, even when they know exactly what they want to say.

[PAUSE]

It is not about intelligence. It is about the blank page. The uncertainty about tone. The worry about getting the words wrong, especially when what you are writing matters — when it is going to a doctor's office, a government department, a landlord, a company that has not done the right thing.

[PAUSE]

AI is very good at this. Not because it writes better than you, but because it removes the blank page problem. You give it the situation. It gives you a draft. You read it, adjust what does not sound like you, and send it. The hard part — knowing what to say and how to say it — is done.

[PAUSE]

This path covers four types of writing:

Formal complaints.
Requests and follow-ups.
Difficult personal messages.
Replies you are not sure how to handle.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Type 1: Formal Complaints"]

Let's start with formal complaints, because they are the most common thing people want help with — and the most likely to produce a result when they are written well.

[PAUSE]

Most people find complaints uncomfortable to write. They either go too soft — "Sorry to bother you, but I was just wondering if maybe..." — or they overcorrect and go too aggressive, which gives the recipient an excuse to dismiss the letter rather than engage with it.

The goal is a complaint that is firm, clear, and professional. Calm authority. Not pleading, not threatening.

[PAUSE]

Here is how to set up the prompt.

[ON SCREEN: Complaint letter prompt]

"I need to write a formal letter of complaint to [organisation] about [problem].

Here is the situation: [brief description — two or three sentences covering what happened, when, and what you have already tried].

I want the letter to be firm and clear, but professional and not aggressive. It should include what I want to happen as a result.

Please draft this as a formal letter, no more than 300 words, ready to send."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

That prompt will produce a letter you can use. You may want to adjust the opening line, or add a specific detail, or change a word or two. But you will have a solid, correctly structured complaint letter in under a minute.

[PAUSE]

A few things worth adding, depending on your situation:

If you have a complaint reference number, include it.
If there is a deadline involved — an appeal window, a billing cycle — mention it.
If previous correspondence has not been responded to, name that: "I have contacted you twice without a response."

All of these details strengthen the letter. And they are easy to include in the prompt rather than trying to work them in yourself afterwards.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Type 2: Requests and Follow-Ups"]

Requests and follow-ups are a different task. You are not complaining — you are asking for something, or chasing up something that has not happened.

[PAUSE]

This category covers a wide range:

Asking a GP for a referral you feel you need.
Following up a quote that a tradesperson gave you three weeks ago.
Writing to your body corporate about a maintenance issue.
Requesting an extension on a bill or a payment plan.
Chasing a government application that seems to have gone quiet.

[PAUSE]

The prompt structure is the same as the complaint letter, with one adjustment: instead of specifying "firm and clear," specify what kind of request this is.

[ON SCREEN: Follow-up request prompt]

"I need to write an email following up on [situation]. I [contacted/applied/submitted] on [date] and have not received a response. I would like to ask [what you want — an update, a decision, next steps]. Please draft this as a polite but clear follow-up email, around 100 to 150 words."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

For requests where you want to be careful about tone — asking for a medical referral, for example, where you do not want to seem demanding — you can specify that:

"I want this to sound collaborative, not demanding. I respect my doctor's judgment but I would like to ask her to consider this."

That instruction will shift the tone significantly.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Type 3: Difficult Personal Messages"]

This is the category people are often most surprised to use AI for. Writing something to a family member about a difficult situation. A message to a friend you have been out of contact with. A note to a neighbour about a problem. A letter to an adult child about something that needs to be said.

[PAUSE]

The reason people resist using AI here is that it feels too personal — too important to delegate to a machine.

But that is a misunderstanding of what you are doing. You are not delegating the message. You are getting help with the words.

You still decide what to say. You decide how honest to be, how much to share, what you want the outcome to be. The AI just helps you find a way to say it that does not come out harsher than you intended, or more apologetic than you wanted, or tangled in qualifications.

[PAUSE]

For difficult personal messages, the most useful prompt element is the one that describes the relationship and what you want the message to do.

[ON SCREEN: Difficult personal message prompt]

"I need to write a message to my [relationship — sister, old friend, neighbour, adult son]. The situation is: [brief description of what has happened and why this needs to be addressed].

What I want to communicate: [the core of what you want to say].
What I am worried about: [the tone risk — I don't want it to sound like blame / I don't want to be dismissive / I'm worried it will seem cold].

Please draft this as a warm but honest personal message, around 150 words. I will read it and adjust anything that does not sound like me."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

Read the draft carefully. Change what does not sound like you. Add the detail that only you would know. The AI gives you a structure and a starting point. You give it a voice.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Type 4: Replies You Are Not Sure How to Handle"]

Sometimes the problem is not writing something from scratch. It is receiving a message — an email from a government agency, a letter from a solicitor, a complaint from a neighbour — and not being sure what to say back.

[PAUSE]

For this, you paste the message in and ask AI to help you think about it.

[ON SCREEN: Received message prompt]

"I have received the following message and I am not sure how to reply. Please read it and tell me:

1. What is this message actually asking me to do or respond to?
2. What are my options for how to respond?
3. Draft a reply that [is professional and does not admit fault / acknowledges the concern but asks for more time / declines politely].

Here is the message: [paste the message]"

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

This approach is useful because it separates the understanding step from the responding step. Before you write anything, you want to be sure you have understood what you have received. The AI is very good at unpacking formal or legalistic language into plain English first.

[PAUSE]

One note of caution: if you have received legal correspondence — something from a solicitor, a court notice, a formal legal claim — use AI to help you understand it, but do not rely on an AI-drafted reply as your response. Those situations warrant legal advice. AI can help you understand what you are dealing with and what questions to ask a solicitor. That is a different and valuable use.

[PAUSE]

Let me bring all four types together.

Formal complaints: firm, clear, professional. Include the reference, the history, what you want.
Requests and follow-ups: polite but clear. Specify the tone carefully.
Difficult personal messages: the relationship, what you want to say, the tone risk.
Replies: paste the message, understand it first, then specify how you want to respond.

[PAUSE]

Each of these follows the same underlying structure from Module 2: context, task, format, tone. You are just applying it to a specific type of writing task.

[PAUSE]

The Cleared To Try for this path: pick one piece of writing you have been putting off. Something in the pile. Draft it using AI this week.

You might be surprised how quickly a task you have been dreading disappears once you have a draft in front of you.
