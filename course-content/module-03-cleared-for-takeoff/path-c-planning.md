# Module 3, Path C: Planning — Trips, Events, Projects, Family

**Spoken duration:** ~14 minutes  
**Word count (spoken):** ~1,850 words  
**Format:** Direct to camera; on-screen prompt examples  
**On-screen text:** Prompt structures; output examples  
**Tone:** Warm, practical, encouraging  

---

[LOOK TO CAMERA]

Planning is one of those tasks that seems straightforward until you are in the middle of it.

[PAUSE]

You know what you want — a trip, a family event, a home project, a milestone celebration — but turning a vague picture into an actual plan involves dozens of small decisions, most of which you did not know you needed to make until they are in front of you.

[PAUSE]

What order do things need to happen in? What are the things I have not thought of yet? Who needs to be involved at which point? What is a realistic budget? What might go wrong?

[PAUSE]

AI is very good at planning. Not because it knows your life, but because it is good at structure. It can take a vague starting point and turn it into a draft plan with logical steps, sensible sequencing, and prompts for the things you have not thought of yet.

[PAUSE]

This path covers four planning scenarios:

Travel and holidays.
Events and celebrations.
Home and renovation projects.
Family and life logistics.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Planning Type 1: Travel and Holidays"]

Let's start with travel, because it is the planning task people have the most enthusiasm for — and where AI can save the most time.

[PAUSE]

The mistake people make with travel planning is asking for too much at once. "Plan me a two-week trip to Japan" produces a generic itinerary that covers the same things every travel website covers. Too vague. Too broad.

[PAUSE]

Better approach: give AI your specific constraints and ask it to solve one part of the planning problem at a time.

[ON SCREEN: Travel planning prompt]

"I am planning a trip to [destination] for [duration], with [number of people]. Here are my constraints:

Budget: [approximate figure, or 'modest' / 'comfortable' / 'not a concern']
Mobility/accessibility: [relevant details, if any]
Travel style: [slow pace vs. seeing a lot / culture-focused / outdoors / relaxation]
Travel dates: [approximate or fixed]
What I already know I want to do: [any fixed points]

Please give me a draft itinerary, broken down by day, with a brief note on what to book in advance versus what can be left flexible."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

That prompt will give you a solid starting point. Then you iterate: "Can you swap day three and day four? I need to be near the airport on the last morning." "What is the most realistic budget for food on this kind of trip?" "We have one person who cannot walk long distances — which parts of this itinerary need to be adjusted?"

[PAUSE]

Each of those follow-ups takes the plan from generic to specific.

[PAUSE]

AI is also useful for the administrative side of travel: packing lists for specific climates, what to know about local transport, what to carry in a document pouch, how to handle medication across international borders. Ask specifically and you get specifically.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Planning Type 2: Events and Celebrations"]

Events have two planning challenges: scope and sequencing.

Scope: what do you actually need to do? Most people underestimate the number of moving parts in an event until they are overwhelmed by them mid-way through the process.

Sequencing: what order do things need to happen? You cannot send invitations before you have the venue. You cannot confirm catering before you know numbers.

[PAUSE]

AI handles both of these well.

[ON SCREEN: Event planning prompt]

"I am organising a [type of event — birthday, reunion, wedding, wake, community dinner] for approximately [number of people] in [location]. The event is on [date or timeframe].

My budget is approximately [figure or range].

Please give me:
1. A complete list of everything I need to organise.
2. A rough timeline working backwards from the event date — what to sort first, what can wait.
3. Any things you would flag as easy to overlook at this stage."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

That third point — easy to overlook — is one of the most useful things to ask AI in any planning context. It surfaces the things that experienced planners know and first-timers discover too late. Parking for guests who need accessible access. A back-up plan for outdoor events in poor weather. Informing the venue that someone in your group has a severe food allergy. The small logistics that no single task list ever seems to include.

[PAUSE]

For a more personal event — a significant birthday, a 50th anniversary celebration, a memorial gathering — you can also ask AI to help with the words. A toast. A tribute. An order of service. These are writing tasks that benefit from the same approach as Path A.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Planning Type 3: Home and Renovation Projects"]

Home projects are planning tasks where the cost of missing something is high. Getting three quotes before you know what scope of work to specify. Starting a renovation without understanding what council approval you might need. Ordering materials before you have confirmed measurements.

[PAUSE]

AI can help you build the planning framework before you spend any money.

[ON SCREEN: Home project prompt]

"I am planning to [project description — renovate the kitchen / replace the bathroom / install a pergola / sort out roof drainage]. I own a [property type — freestanding house / apartment / unit in a complex] in [state].

I have not started getting quotes yet. Before I do, please help me:

1. Understand the typical scope of this type of project — what is usually involved.
2. Identify what decisions I need to make before I can brief a tradesperson.
3. Flag any permits, council approvals, or body corporate considerations I should check.
4. Give me a list of questions to ask when getting quotes."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

That last point — questions to ask when getting quotes — is particularly valuable. Most people walk into a quote without a clear brief, and end up comparing apples with oranges because different tradespeople have scoped the work differently.

Going in with a specific question list means you can get consistent quotes and ask the same questions of everyone.

[PAUSE]

AI can also help with the budget side: "What is a realistic cost range for a kitchen renovation in Australia in 2025?" It will give you a range with appropriate caveats about variability. That range is useful as a sanity check — not a guarantee, but a reference point before you start talking to anyone.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Planning Type 4: Family and Life Logistics"]

This is the broadest category, and probably the one most people have not thought to use AI for.

[PAUSE]

Family logistics includes:

Coordinating care arrangements — for a parent, a child, or a family member with support needs.
Managing an estate after a death — the checklist of what needs to happen.
Preparing for a hospital stay — your own or a family member's.
Thinking through a major life transition — moving house, retirement, a child leaving home.
Working out how to have a family conversation about a difficult topic — money, care, a significant decision.

[PAUSE]

For any of these, AI's value is in the structure it brings. You often know the situation better than anyone. What you lack is a clear sequence and a list of things to think about.

[PAUSE]

One example that comes up often: managing a parent's affairs when they become unwell. There are practical tasks — medical decisions, care arrangements, finances, legal documents. There are family dynamics. There are things that feel urgent and things that can wait. It is easy to feel overwhelmed.

[ON SCREEN: Life logistics prompt]

"I am helping manage a situation where [brief description — my mother has just been diagnosed with dementia / my father has moved into residential care / my partner is having surgery]. I need to work out what I need to do over the next [timeframe].

Please help me build a practical checklist of things to consider, broken into:
- Immediate priorities (this week)
- Short-term (next month)
- Longer-term (ongoing)

Include any legal, medical, financial, or government considerations I should look into. Flag anything where I should be getting professional advice."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

That prompt produces a structured starting point. You will adapt it heavily to your specific situation — but having the structure means you are not trying to hold everything in your head at once.

[PAUSE]

For all four planning types, the same principle holds: start with the broad picture, then narrow. Let the first response give you structure. Then go through it section by section, adjusting, adding, removing.

[PAUSE]

Planning is not about perfection on the first pass. It is about having something to work from. AI gives you that something very quickly.

[PAUSE]

Your Cleared To Try for this path: pick one thing you are planning — a trip, an event, a project, a logistics challenge — and spend thirty minutes using AI to build a draft plan. Do not aim for a finished plan. Aim for a starting point.
