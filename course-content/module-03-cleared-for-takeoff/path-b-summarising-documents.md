# Module 3, Path B: Summarising Documents

**Spoken duration:** ~13 minutes  
**Word count (spoken):** ~1,750 words  
**Format:** Direct to camera; on-screen examples of prompts  
**On-screen text:** Prompt structures; output examples  
**Tone:** Practical, reassuring, direct  

---

[LOOK TO CAMERA]

There is a particular kind of dread that comes from opening a document you know you have to read and understand — and realising it is thirty pages long, written in formal language you are not familiar with, and contains information that actually matters to you.

[PAUSE]

A lease agreement. A superannuation statement. An insurance policy document. A medical letter from a specialist. A legal document someone has asked you to sign. A government decision letter that seems to be important but is written in a way that makes it almost impossible to follow.

[PAUSE]

Most people read these twice, slowly, and still walk away unsure whether they understood them correctly. Some people set them aside and hope they will feel clearer later. Some people call someone who might know more — if they can think of who to call.

[PAUSE]

AI changes this completely.

[PAUSE]

If you can copy and paste the text of a document, or type the key sections into a chat window, you can ask AI to translate it into plain English for you. Not as a substitute for professional advice when that is what you need — but as a first step that gets you from confused to informed.

[PAUSE]

This path covers four types of document situations:

Long documents you need to understand quickly.
Documents with specific information you are trying to find.
Documents you need to compare.
Documents that require you to do something — and you need to know what.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Situation 1: I need to understand this document"]

Let's start with the general-purpose situation: you have a document, you need to understand it, you are not sure where to start.

[PAUSE]

The prompt I use for this:

[ON SCREEN: Basic summary prompt]

"I am going to paste a document below. Please:

1. Tell me in plain English what this document is and what it is about.
2. Summarise the main points — what it is saying or requiring.
3. Tell me if there are any deadlines, actions, or decisions I need to take.
4. Flag anything that looks unusual, significant, or that I should ask someone about.

Keep the summary under 250 words. Use plain language — assume I am not a specialist in this area.

[Paste document here]"

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

That four-part structure does something specific: it separates what the document is (context) from what it is asking of you (action). Most documents have both, and most people's confusion is about the second part — what am I actually supposed to do with this?

[PAUSE]

After the summary, you can follow up. "You mentioned there's a deadline in section three. Can you pull that out and tell me exactly what I need to do and by when?" That kind of targeted follow-up turns a general summary into an action plan.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Situation 2: I need to find specific information"]

Sometimes you do not need to understand the whole document. You need to find one thing in it. A figure. A clause. A condition.

[PAUSE]

"What does my insurance policy say about flood damage?"
"Is there a clause about early exit fees?"
"What is the notice period I need to give under this lease?"

[PAUSE]

The prompt for this is simpler:

[ON SCREEN: Find specific information prompt]

"I am going to paste a document below. I need you to find and explain [specific thing I am looking for]. Please quote the relevant section, then explain what it means in plain English.

[Paste document]"

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

The instruction to quote the relevant section and then explain it is important. You want to see the actual words the document uses — because those words are what will matter if there is ever a dispute — and you want a plain-English explanation of what those words actually mean.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Situation 3: I need to compare two documents"]

This comes up more than you might expect. Two insurance quotes. Two lease options. Two versions of a contract — one you received and one that has been revised. Two different superannuation funds' product disclosure statements.

[PAUSE]

Comparing documents side by side is time-consuming and easy to get wrong. You can paste both documents — or the relevant sections — and ask for a comparison.

[ON SCREEN: Comparison prompt]

"I am going to paste two documents. Please compare them and tell me:

1. What are the main differences between the two?
2. Which is better for me, based on [what matters to you — lowest cost / fewest restrictions / longest notice period]?
3. Are there any clauses or conditions in either one I should pay particular attention to?

Document 1: [paste]
Document 2: [paste]"

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

For a comparison task, be specific about what matters to you. "Which is better for me" without context produces a generic analysis. "Which is better for me if I value flexibility and low exit costs" produces something genuinely useful.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Situation 4: What do I need to do?"]

This is a specific type of document that deserves its own prompt structure: documents that are asking you to take action, respond, make a decision, or meet a deadline.

Government notices. Legal correspondence. Insurance claims processes. Application requirements. Tax documentation.

[PAUSE]

For these, you want a clear action list, not just a summary.

[ON SCREEN: Action-focused prompt]

"I am going to paste a document below. I need to understand:

1. What is this asking me to do?
2. What are the exact steps I need to take?
3. Are there any deadlines?
4. What happens if I do not respond or take action?

Please give me this as a numbered action list, in plain language, in the order I need to do things.

[Paste document]"

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

The numbered action list format is important here. You are not looking for narrative. You want to be able to work through the steps one at a time without re-reading the summary every time.

[PAUSE]

A few notes before we move on.

[PAUSE]

On privacy: if a document contains sensitive personal information — a medical record, bank account details, a full legal identity document — think carefully before pasting the entire thing. You can often paste only the relevant section. Or you can describe the situation in general terms and ask the AI to explain how that type of document typically works, then apply the explanation to what you are reading.

[PAUSE]

On accuracy: AI will usually give you a good, plain-English summary of a document's contents. But it can miss nuance, or occasionally misread a specific clause. If a document has significant legal or financial implications — a contract you are about to sign, an insurance policy you are about to rely on — treat the AI summary as a starting point, not a final word. Use it to know what questions to ask a professional.

[PAUSE]

On long documents: most AI tools have a limit on how much text you can paste in one message. For very long documents, paste the most relevant sections, or break it into parts. You can say "I am going to send this in three sections" and work through it sequentially.

[PAUSE]

The difference this makes in practice is not small. A document that would have sat in a pile for two weeks because it felt too hard to tackle becomes a ten-minute task. You understand what it is. You know what you need to do. You can ask the right questions if you need professional advice.

[PAUSE]

That is not a small thing. That is a real change in what feels manageable.

[PAUSE]

Your Cleared To Try for this path: find a document that has been sitting in a pile — physical or digital — that you have been avoiding because it felt too complicated. Paste the relevant section into AI and ask it to tell you, in plain English, what you actually need to do.
