# Module 3, Path D: Research and Fact-Finding

**Spoken duration:** ~14 minutes  
**Word count (spoken):** ~1,850 words  
**Format:** Direct to camera; on-screen examples  
**On-screen text:** Prompt structures; verification checklist  
**Tone:** Clear, practical, honest about limitations  

---

[LOOK TO CAMERA]

Research is one of the things AI does well — with one important qualification.

[PAUSE]

AI does not have access to the internet in real time. It was trained on information up to a certain date, and that training does not update daily. So when you ask it what the current interest rate is, or who currently leads a particular government department, or what a piece of legislation says right now — it may give you information that was accurate a year ago and is no longer.

[PAUSE]

This is not a reason to avoid using AI for research. It is a reason to understand what kind of research it is good for, and what kind of verification habits go alongside it.

[PAUSE]

Let me break this into four types of research:

Understanding how something works.
Comparing options.
Finding out what questions to ask.
Getting background before an appointment or meeting.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Research Type 1: Understanding How Something Works"]

This is where AI is strongest — and where the real-time limitation matters least.

[PAUSE]

Understanding how something works — how superannuation drawdown works, how capital gains tax is calculated, how a body corporate operates, how aged care funding works in Australia, how a probate process unfolds — this is the kind of knowledge that does not change week to week.

The frameworks are stable. The principles are stable. The general processes are stable. The specific rates and thresholds may change annually, but the way the system works does not.

[PAUSE]

AI can explain complex systems in plain English better than almost any other source, because you can ask it to match the explanation to your level.

[ON SCREEN: Explanation prompt]

"Can you explain how [topic] works in Australia? I have no background in this area. Please use plain language, avoid jargon, and explain any terms you use. I want to understand the basics well enough to know what questions I should be asking."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

That last line — "well enough to know what questions I should be asking" — is important. You are not asking AI to replace your accountant or financial adviser. You are asking it to get you to the point where you can have a productive conversation with one.

That is a genuinely valuable use. Walking into a professional appointment already understanding the basics means you get more out of the time, ask better questions, and are less likely to be confused by the answers.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Research Type 2: Comparing Options"]

Comparison is another strong suit.

[PAUSE]

What is the difference between term life insurance and income protection insurance?
What are the pros and cons of a self-managed super fund versus an industry fund?
What does a financial power of attorney cover that a general power of attorney does not?
What is the difference between palliative care and residential aged care?

[PAUSE]

These are the kinds of questions people search the internet for and find overwhelming — because the internet returns marketing pages, sponsored content, and articles written for specialists.

AI gives you a clear, structured comparison in plain language, tailored to the level of explanation you need.

[ON SCREEN: Comparison research prompt]

"Can you compare [option A] and [option B] in plain language? I want to understand:

1. What each one is.
2. The main differences between them.
3. Who typically benefits more from each one.
4. What questions I should be asking before deciding between them.

Please assume I have no specialist knowledge in this area."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

Again, the verification habit here: for anything where the specific current details matter — current rates, current thresholds, current eligibility criteria — check a live source after you have got the framework from AI.

The ATO website. The Services Australia website. The ASIC MoneySmart website. Medicare. Your state government's relevant department.

AI gives you the context to understand what you are looking at. The live source gives you the current numbers.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Research Type 3: Finding Out What Questions to Ask"]

This is one of AI's most underused capabilities, and one of its most genuinely useful ones.

[PAUSE]

When you are about to enter an unfamiliar situation — a conversation with a financial planner, a meeting with a solicitor, a first appointment with a specialist, a call with your super fund, a session at a Centrelink office — you often do not know enough to know what to ask.

[PAUSE]

AI can help you prepare.

[ON SCREEN: Questions preparation prompt]

"I have an appointment coming up with [type of professional] to discuss [topic]. I am not familiar with this area and I want to make the most of the time.

Please give me:
1. A list of questions I should ask.
2. Any information I should bring or prepare in advance.
3. Things to watch out for or ask to have explained if they come up.

My situation is: [brief relevant context]."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

Arriving at a professional appointment with a prepared question list changes the dynamic of the meeting. You get more useful information. You feel more in control. You leave with a clearer sense of what comes next.

And the question list takes about five minutes to generate.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Research Type 4: Background Before a Decision"]

The fourth type of research is background before a significant decision. Not comparison, not explanation — but the full picture of a situation before you make a call.

[PAUSE]

You are thinking about moving to a new area — what should you know about that area before committing?
You have been offered a financial product you are not familiar with — what are the standard things to look for and look out for?
You are considering a particular type of medical procedure — what are the typical questions patients ask, and what does the process usually look like?
You are about to negotiate a lease renewal — what are your rights as a tenant in your state?

[PAUSE]

For this type of research:

[ON SCREEN: Background research prompt]

"I am about to [make a decision / enter a situation] and I want to understand the full picture first. 

The situation is: [describe it].

Please help me understand:
1. The key things I should know before proceeding.
2. The common mistakes people make in this situation.
3. What a good outcome looks like and what a poor one looks like.
4. Where I should verify the information you give me, since I want to make sure I have current, accurate details."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

That last point — asking AI where to verify its information — is a habit worth building. A good AI response will often tell you: "For current rates, check the ATO website. For your specific state's tenancy rules, check the relevant tenancy authority." That kind of guidance from the AI itself helps you build verification into the process rather than bolting it on at the end.

[PAUSE]

Let me be direct about the verification habit, because it is the thing that separates confident AI users from ones who get into trouble.

[PAUSE]

For anything that involves:

A specific dollar figure (rates, thresholds, fees, charges)
Current eligibility rules for a government scheme
Your specific legal rights under state law
Current medical guidelines or treatment protocols

Go to a primary source to confirm the current details.

[PAUSE]

AI is the researcher who gives you the framework and the context. The live official source is where you verify the specifics.

Using both together is more effective than either alone. The AI helps you know what you are looking for. The official source tells you whether the current figures match what AI described.

[PAUSE]

One practical tip: when you find a piece of current information on an official website, paste it back into AI and ask it to help you understand what it means. "I found this on the ATO website: [paste the text]. Can you explain what this means for my situation, which is [brief context]?"

That back-and-forth between AI and live sources is a genuinely powerful research approach. You are using each one for what it does best.

[PAUSE]

Your Cleared To Try for this path: pick one topic you have been meaning to understand better — something financial, legal, medical, or administrative — and spend thirty minutes using AI to build your understanding. Then check one specific current figure or rule on the relevant official website.
