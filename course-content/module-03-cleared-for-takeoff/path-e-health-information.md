# Module 3, Path E: Health Information

**Spoken duration:** ~14 minutes  
**Word count (spoken):** ~1,850 words  
**Format:** Direct to camera; on-screen prompt examples  
**On-screen text:** Prompt structures; what AI can and cannot do  
**Tone:** Warm, direct, frank about limitations — not alarming  

---

[LOOK TO CAMERA]

I want to be upfront about something at the start of this path.

[PAUSE]

AI is not a doctor. It is not a substitute for medical advice. If you are experiencing symptoms that worry you, the right first step is still to call your GP, go to an urgent care clinic, or call 000 if it is an emergency.

[PAUSE]

That said — there is a significant gap between "seeing a doctor" and "completely uninformed," and AI can help you fill that gap in ways that are genuinely useful.

[PAUSE]

Understanding a diagnosis you have just received.
Making sense of a medical letter or specialist report.
Knowing what questions to ask at your next appointment.
Understanding a medication — what it does, why it has been prescribed, what the common side effects are.
Getting a clearer picture of a condition that affects you or someone you care for.
Understanding what a procedure involves before it happens.

[PAUSE]

None of these replace your doctor. All of them help you be a better-informed patient. And being a better-informed patient — being able to ask clear questions, understand the answers, and advocate for yourself — matters.

[PAUSE]

This path covers five types of health-related AI use:

Understanding what you have been told.
Preparing for appointments.
Understanding medications.
Supporting a carer or family member.
Navigating the health system.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Health Use 1: Understanding What You Have Been Told"]

Doctors and specialists speak in a language developed for professionals. Most of them do their best to explain things clearly, but there is a lot of ground to cover in a short appointment, and it is very easy to walk out with information you understood in the room and cannot quite reconstruct two hours later.

[PAUSE]

You can use AI to reconstruct and deepen your understanding after the fact.

[ON SCREEN: Understanding a diagnosis prompt]

"I have just been told I have [condition / diagnosis]. I am not very familiar with this. Can you explain to me, in plain language:

1. What this condition is.
2. What typically causes it or contributes to it.
3. What the usual management or treatment approach is.
4. What questions I should go back to my doctor with, now that I have had some time to think.

I am not looking for medical advice about my specific case. I want to understand the general landscape so I can have a better conversation with my doctor."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

That last sentence matters. It is not mealy-mouthed. It tells the AI what role you are asking it to play — and it keeps you in the right frame of mind too. You are seeking understanding, not a diagnosis or a treatment plan.

[PAUSE]

If you have a medical letter or specialist report you want to understand, you can paste the relevant section and ask AI to explain what it says in plain English. "The letter says [paste text]. Can you explain what this means?"

[PAUSE]

One important caution: do not paste documents that contain your full name, date of birth, Medicare number, or other identifying information. Paste only the clinical sections. Keep your personal identifying details out of the AI chat.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Health Use 2: Preparing for Appointments"]

This is one of the highest-value uses of AI in a health context, and one of the least obvious.

[PAUSE]

Most people go to a doctor's appointment with a vague sense of what they want to discuss, get in the room, and find that either the conversation goes a different direction, or they forget to raise the things that mattered to them.

[PAUSE]

Five minutes with AI before the appointment changes this.

[ON SCREEN: Appointment preparation prompt]

"I have an appointment with my [GP / cardiologist / rheumatologist / other specialist] to discuss [condition or topic]. 

My concerns going in are: [list your main concerns].
I want to ask about: [any specific things — test results, a medication change, a procedure you have heard about].

Please help me:
1. Organise my thoughts into a clear, prioritised list of things to raise.
2. Suggest any questions I should ask that I might not have thought of.
3. Tell me if there is any information I should bring to the appointment."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

You can print or save this list and take it into the appointment. You do not have to follow it rigidly — but having it in front of you means you will not forget the thing that mattered most when you are in the room.

[PAUSE]

After the appointment, you can use AI to process what you were told. "I was told today that [description of what the doctor said]. Can you help me understand what this means and what my next steps are?"

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Health Use 3: Understanding Medications"]

Most people have had the experience of being prescribed a medication they know nothing about, reading the consumer medicine information leaflet (which is written for regulatory purposes, not for patients), and coming away more anxious than before.

[PAUSE]

AI is very good at explaining medications in plain, sensible language.

[ON SCREEN: Medication question prompt]

"I have been prescribed [medication name]. Can you explain to me:

1. What this medication is for.
2. How it works in general terms.
3. What the common side effects are.
4. What I should watch for that would be worth raising with my doctor.
5. Anything I should avoid while taking it — food, other medications, activities.

I will discuss any specific concerns with my doctor or pharmacist. I just want to understand the basics."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

Your pharmacist is also an excellent resource for medication questions — and underused. But AI can give you a plain-English starting point that makes that conversation with the pharmacist more productive.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Health Use 4: Supporting a Carer or Family Member"]

Many people using this course are caring for someone else as well as managing their own health. An ageing parent. A partner with a chronic condition. A family member with a disability. Caring involves its own learning curve — medical, practical, and emotional.

[PAUSE]

AI can help carers in several ways:

Understanding the condition you are helping manage.
Finding out what services and supports are available.
Preparing for medical appointments on someone else's behalf.
Understanding your rights as a carer — respite care, carer payments, support services.
Working out how to have difficult conversations about care with the person you support, or with other family members.

[PAUSE]

For this, the same prompt structures apply. The key addition is being explicit that you are asking in a carer context:

"I am the primary carer for my [relationship — mother, husband, brother] who has [condition]. I am trying to understand..."

That context changes the kind of information AI surfaces.

[PAUSE]

For carers specifically: Carer Gateway is the Australian government's dedicated support service. AI can explain what it offers, but going to the Carer Gateway website directly — carersgateway.gov.au — will give you current, accurate information about services in your area.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Health Use 5: Navigating the Health System"]

The Australian health system is genuinely complex. Private and public hospitals. Bulk-billing versus gap fees. Medicare. Private health insurance. Specialists and referrals. Aged care assessments. NDIS.

[PAUSE]

Understanding how these systems work — and how they interact with each other — is a legitimate use of AI.

[ON SCREEN: Health system navigation prompt]

"Can you explain how [part of the health system] works in Australia? I want to understand [specific aspect — what a referral pathway typically looks like / how aged care funding is assessed / how to access a specialist without a long wait / what Medicare covers for [type of service]].

Please use plain language and flag anything where I should check for current information on the relevant government website."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

The health system is an area where current information matters. Thresholds, rebate amounts, eligibility criteria — these change. Use AI to understand the structure and process, then check the relevant government website for current figures.

Medicare: servicesaustralia.gov.au
Aged care: myagedcare.gov.au
NDIS: ndis.gov.au
Private health insurance: privatehealth.gov.au

[PAUSE]

One last thought before we close this path.

[PAUSE]

People sometimes feel embarrassed to look up medical information because they are worried about catastrophising, or being told something that frightens them, or being seen as a hypochondriac.

[PAUSE]

Understanding your health is not hypochondria. Wanting to know what is happening in your body, what treatments are available, what your options are — that is appropriate self-advocacy. It is taking your own health seriously.

AI can help you build that understanding without the spiral that sometimes comes from typing symptoms into a search engine at midnight. Use it as an informed starting point, not a final verdict.

[PAUSE]

Your Cleared To Try for this path: use AI to prepare for your next health appointment. Write down your concerns beforehand, ask AI to help you organise them into a clear list, and take that list into the room with you.
