# Module 4, Track B: Start Something

**Spoken duration:** ~25 minutes  
**Word count (spoken):** ~3,200 words  
**Format:** Direct to camera; on-screen examples  
**On-screen text:** Prompt structures; worked examples  
**Tone:** Practical, encouraging, honest about what starting involves  

---

[LOOK TO CAMERA]

Welcome to the starting track.

[PAUSE]

You have an idea. Maybe it has been sitting with you for years. A small business. A way to generate some income from a skill you have. A community group. A newsletter. Something you want to offer that you have not quite worked out how to package or launch.

[PAUSE]

The gap between having an idea and doing something with it is almost always the same gap: not knowing what the first steps are, or feeling like the first steps are too large to take alone.

[PAUSE]

AI will not do the work for you. It will not talk to your first customers, build your product, or make the calls. But it is a remarkably good thinking partner for the early stages of starting something — when you need to pressure-test ideas, understand a space you are not fully familiar with, and work out what to do first.

[PAUSE]

This track covers five stages of starting something:

Pressure-testing the idea.
Understanding the landscape.
Working out the first steps.
Building the basics — name, offer, message.
Starting in public — if that is what you want to do.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Stage 1: Pressure-Testing the Idea"]

Most ideas feel either too obvious or too uncertain until they have been examined from the outside.

[PAUSE]

AI can act as a thinking partner who asks the uncomfortable questions early — before you have spent time and money — in a way that is honest without being discouraging.

[ON SCREEN: Idea pressure-test prompt]

"I have an idea for [brief description of what you want to start]. I want to pressure-test it.

Please:
1. Tell me what is strong about this idea — where you can see it working.
2. Tell me the genuine risks or weaknesses — what could go wrong, what could be harder than I expect.
3. Ask me questions that I should be able to answer before I go further.

Do not just be supportive. I want honest analysis."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

That last instruction matters. AI has a tendency to be encouraging. You want the sceptical version of the response as well as the supportive one — not because the idea is bad, but because the questions it raises are the ones you will need to answer anyway.

[PAUSE]

After that analysis, you can choose how to respond to the risks and weaknesses. Some will be things you had already thought through. Others will be genuinely useful to address before you proceed.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Stage 2: Understanding the Landscape"]

Before you start, it is useful to understand what already exists in the space.

[PAUSE]

Who else is doing something similar? What are they offering and at what price? What is the gap — what are they not doing that you could? What does success look like in this space and what does failure look like?

[ON SCREEN: Landscape research prompt]

"I want to understand the landscape for [what you are starting]. Can you help me understand:

1. What similar offerings already exist — in Australia and elsewhere.
2. What seems to work well in this space.
3. Where the gaps are — what is not being done, or not being done well.
4. What the typical business models look like — how do others charge for this?
5. Any factors specific to the Australian market I should be aware of.

Please be specific where you can, and flag where I should do my own research to verify current information."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

AI's knowledge of specific businesses and current market conditions will not always be current. Use its response as a starting framework, then do your own verification — look at what is currently operating, search for recent articles, and if possible, talk to people already in the space.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Stage 3: Working Out the First Steps"]

One of the most paralysing aspects of starting something is not knowing what order to do things in.

[PAUSE]

People try to do everything at once, or they do things in the wrong order — building a website before they have a single customer, or designing a logo before they know what they are selling.

[ON SCREEN: First steps prompt]

"I want to start [what you are starting]. I have [description of where you are — an idea but nothing built / some experience in this area / an existing skill I want to commercialise / a small number of potential customers].

What are the first five to ten steps I should take, in order? Please sequence them logically — don't list things I should do before other things are in place.

Flag which steps I need professional advice on — legal, financial, tax — so I know where to get expert input rather than just figuring it out myself."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

The sequencing instruction is the key. Most starting-something checklists are comprehensive but not sequenced. Knowing not just what to do but what to do first is what makes the list actually useful.

[PAUSE]

The flag about professional advice is equally important. Starting a business in Australia involves decisions with real legal and financial implications — business structure, ABN registration, GST thresholds, insurance. These are not areas to navigate purely through AI. A quick conversation with an accountant or business adviser at the right moment can save significant problems later.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Stage 4: Building the Basics"]

The basics of any small offering are three things: what it is called, what it offers, and how it is described to someone who has never heard of it.

[PAUSE]

Let's take each one.

[ON SCREEN: Name brainstorm prompt]

"I am starting [what you are starting]. I need help thinking through what to call it.

What I want the name to convey: [qualities — trustworthy, warm, professional, local, creative, etc.].
What I want to avoid: [overly corporate / too clever / hard to spell or remember].
My target customers are: [brief description].

Please give me fifteen name options across a range of approaches — some literal, some more evocative, some that use my own name. Then tell me which three you think are strongest and why."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

Do not get stuck on the name. Naming is important but it is not the thing that makes or breaks something. Pick a name you can live with, register an ABN and a domain if you need one, and keep moving.

[PAUSE]

For the offer — what you are actually selling or providing — be as specific as possible:

[ON SCREEN: Offer clarity prompt]

"I am offering [description of what you do]. I need to describe this clearly so a potential customer immediately understands:

1. What they get.
2. Who this is for.
3. Why it is worth paying for.

Please help me draft a one-paragraph description of my offer. Then give me a shorter one-sentence version I could use as a tagline or introduction."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

Read both versions. If the paragraph does not feel like something you would say about your own work, change it until it does. The language you use with actual customers should feel natural to you — not like marketing copy.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Stage 5: Starting in Public"]

Not everything you start needs to be in public. Some things are private: an income-generating side skill, a service offered to people you already know, a community group with a defined membership.

[PAUSE]

But if part of your idea involves being visible — running a newsletter, writing a blog, building a social media presence, starting a Substack, establishing yourself as someone with something to say — then AI can help significantly with the content side.

[PAUSE]

The first thing to establish is what you are going to say, and who you are saying it to.

[ON SCREEN: Content positioning prompt]

"I want to start [type of content — a newsletter / Substack / social media presence / blog] about [topic]. 

My perspective is: [what is specific to you — your experience, your point of view, what you know that others might not].
My intended audience is: [who you want to reach].
What I want them to get from it: [what value you want to provide].

Please help me:
1. Define what makes my take on this topic worth following.
2. Draft an introductory post that explains what I am doing and why.
3. Suggest ten potential topics for future posts."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

The ten topic suggestions are a starting inventory. You do not have to use all of them or use them in that order. But having a list means you never sit down to write with a blank slate.

[PAUSE]

A note on authenticity: AI-generated content that is published as if it is your own writing is a version of dishonesty that readers eventually detect. The most effective use of AI in a content context is not to generate content for you to publish unchanged. It is to:

Help you work out what to say.
Give you a first draft you then revise substantially.
Edit your drafts for clarity.
Help you come up with ideas when you are stuck.

Your voice, your perspective, your experience — those are what make content worth reading. AI can support all of that without replacing it.

[PAUSE]

One specific starting scenario worth covering: you have a skill or service you want to offer locally. A few hours of bookkeeping help. Dog walking. Gardening. Tech support for older neighbours. Decluttering assistance. Tutoring.

For this, AI can help you:

Draft a clear description of your service for a local Facebook group or noticeboard.
Write a short professional message to potential customers who know you.
Prepare a simple price list with clear descriptions of what is included.
Think through how to handle common practical questions — availability, payment, cancellations.

[ON SCREEN: Local service prompt]

"I want to offer [service] locally. My target customers are [description — retirees / families in my suburb / small businesses]. My experience is [brief description].

Please help me:
1. Draft a short post I could put on a local community Facebook group.
2. Suggest a simple price structure.
3. Give me a list of practical questions I should think through before I start — availability, payment, what happens if I am sick, etc."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

Starting small and local is one of the most sustainable ways to begin. You learn what you are offering, how people respond to it, and what you need to refine — without large investment or risk.

[PAUSE]

Before we close this track: the single biggest obstacle to starting something is not information or resources. It is the moment between deciding to start and taking the first visible action.

[PAUSE]

AI can help you with the thinking. But the first action has to come from you.

[PAUSE]

Your Cleared To Try for this track: this week, take one concrete first step on your idea. Not a thinking step — a doing step. Write the offer. Register the domain. Send the first message to a potential customer. Post in the local group. Spend two hours working on something that will exist at the end of the session.
