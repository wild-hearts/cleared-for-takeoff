# Module 4, Track C: Sort Something

**Spoken duration:** ~25 minutes  
**Word count (spoken):** ~3,200 words  
**Format:** Direct to camera; on-screen examples  
**On-screen text:** Prompt structures; worked examples  
**Tone:** Practical, direct, matter-of-fact — acknowledges that "sorting" is often emotionally weighty  

---

[LOOK TO CAMERA]

Welcome to the sorting track.

[PAUSE]

If you chose this track, there is probably something specific in your life that needs to be sorted. Not a small thing — a significant one. Something that has been sitting in the background, half-addressed or not addressed at all, because it involves many moving parts, because it is emotionally heavy, or because you simply have not found the time and the energy at the same moment.

[PAUSE]

Sorting something — really sorting it, not just making a list and putting it down — requires a structure you can work through. The problem is that these are usually the exact situations where structure is hardest to find on your own, because the size of the thing makes it hard to see clearly.

[PAUSE]

AI is a particularly good tool for this kind of work. Not because it knows your situation better than you do, but because it is good at breaking large, tangled things into sequences of smaller steps — and at helping you see what you need to address first.

[PAUSE]

This track covers four types of significant sorting:

Retirement planning and the transition out of work.
A major renovation or property decision.
A significant life administration task.
A trip or experience you have been putting off for too long.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Sorting Type 1: Retirement and the Transition Out of Work"]

Retirement is one of the most significant transitions most people make. And unlike most transitions, it involves decisions in multiple domains simultaneously: financial, practical, social, and personal.

[PAUSE]

Financial: super, age pension, assets, income, tax, estate planning.
Practical: what you do with your time, what structure you have, how you stay connected.
Social: relationships that were work-based, new communities, how retirement changes your partnerships and friendships.
Personal: identity, purpose, what this stage of life is for.

[PAUSE]

Most retirement planning treats only the first of these. The financial plan. Which is important — but it is not the whole plan.

[ON SCREEN: Retirement planning prompt]

"I am [approaching retirement / recently retired / thinking about retirement in the next few years]. I want to think through this transition properly.

My situation: [brief description — age, whether still working, partner's situation if relevant, financial starting point, health considerations].

I want a complete picture of what I need to think through — not just the finances. Please cover:
1. Financial planning — what I need to know and do.
2. Practical planning — daily structure, roles and activities.
3. Social and relationship considerations.
4. Personal questions worth sitting with.

And for each area, tell me: what decisions do I need to make? What can wait? What should I be getting professional advice on?"

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

The financial side of retirement involves complex decisions where professional advice genuinely matters. A financial planner who specialises in retirement can model your specific situation in ways AI cannot. But AI can help you arrive at that conversation already clear about your situation and your questions.

[PAUSE]

For the non-financial aspects of retirement — which are often the harder ones — AI can help you think through what structure, purpose, and connection look like for you specifically.

"I have retired and I am finding the unstructured time harder than I expected. Can you help me think through how to build a satisfying daily and weekly structure? My interests include [list]. I have [describe any existing commitments or obligations]. I want to stay [active / socially connected / mentally engaged / all of these]."

[PAUSE]

That is a real conversation worth having. And AI will ask you useful questions in return.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Sorting Type 2: Renovation and Property Decisions"]

Renovation projects sit at the intersection of practical planning, financial decisions, and — often — significant stress. They involve decisions made under uncertainty, professionals whose advice sometimes conflicts, councils and regulations, and a timeline that rarely goes to plan.

[PAUSE]

AI will not tell you what your renovation will cost or whether your builder is reputable. But it can help you build the planning framework that goes into a project well-prepared.

[ON SCREEN: Renovation planning prompt]

"I am planning to [description of project — renovate my kitchen / build an extension / update the bathroom / sort out the drainage / refresh the whole house before selling].

I own a [property type — house / apartment / strata unit] in [state].

Before I start getting quotes, I need to:
1. Understand the typical scope of this project — what is usually involved that people often do not anticipate.
2. Understand what council approvals or permits I might need.
3. Know what decisions I need to make before I can brief a builder or tradesperson.
4. Have a list of questions to ask when getting quotes so I can compare them properly.

Please give me this as a structured guide."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

That fourth point — questions for quotes — is the one most people skip, and it is the one that matters most. If you ask three builders for quotes without a consistent brief, you will get three incomparable quotes. If you ask the same questions of all three, you can make an informed decision.

[PAUSE]

For a decision about whether to renovate or sell — one of the most common property dilemmas — AI can help you structure the thinking:

"I am trying to decide whether to renovate my current home or sell and buy something more suitable. My situation is: [describe it — your age, your home's current state, the market in your area, what you would need from a new home]. Please help me think through this decision. What are the key factors? What questions should I be asking? What would I need to know to make a well-informed decision either way?"

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Sorting Type 3: Life Administration"]

Life administration covers a broad territory — the significant practical tasks that accumulate and are never quite urgent enough to address, until something happens that makes them urgent.

[PAUSE]

This includes:

Getting your legal documents in order — will, power of attorney, advance care directive.
Organising your estate so it is manageable for whoever handles it after you.
Sorting finances that have become complicated — multiple accounts, old super funds, unused insurance.
Helping an ageing parent get their affairs in order.
Recovering from a life change — a divorce, a death, a major illness — that has left the administration in disarray.

[PAUSE]

For any significant life administration task, the most useful starting prompt is a comprehensive one:

[ON SCREEN: Life administration prompt]

"I want to sort out my [describe it — will and legal documents / estate planning / financial administration / parent's affairs]. I have been putting this off and I want to approach it systematically.

My situation: [brief relevant description].

Please give me:
1. A complete checklist of everything that needs to be addressed.
2. A suggested order — what to do first, what follows from what.
3. Which steps require professional advice — a solicitor, a financial adviser, an accountant.
4. Which steps I can complete myself, and how.
5. Any time-sensitive items I should address first."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

For wills and legal documents specifically: you should use a solicitor. AI can explain what these documents are, what they cover, and what decisions you need to make before your appointment. It can help you understand the difference between a power of attorney and an advance care directive. It can help you think through who you want to appoint for what role. All of that preparation makes the solicitor appointment significantly more efficient.

But the documents themselves need to be prepared by a qualified professional. This is not an area to DIY.

[PAUSE]

For estate administration after a death — working through what needs to happen when someone you love has died — AI can be a helpful guide through a process that is difficult on multiple levels:

"My [relationship] has just passed away and I need to understand what has to be done to handle their estate. I am [executor of the will / next of kin / both]. My situation is [brief description — whether there is a will, whether the estate is complex, whether there are other family members involved].

Please give me a practical guide to what needs to happen, in roughly the right order, and tell me which parts require a solicitor or other professional."

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Sorting Type 4: The Trip You Keep Putting Off"]

Sometimes sorting something is not administrative. It is a trip, an experience, or a goal that matters to you — that has been sitting in the "someday" category for too long.

[PAUSE]

A journey that means something. Visiting a place that has significance. A family trip that involves complex logistics. An experience — a long walk, a creative retreat, a journey to a place where family came from.

[PAUSE]

The reason these trips do not happen is rarely that they are impossible. It is that the planning feels overwhelming, or there are too many decisions to make before you can commit, or the right time never quite arrives.

[PAUSE]

AI can help you break the planning into manageable pieces — and sometimes, simply helping you get the plan to a state of "this is actually doable" is what gets the trip off the list and into the calendar.

[ON SCREEN: Meaningful trip prompt]

"I want to take a trip to [destination / type of experience] that I have been thinking about for a long time. Here is what I know:

What I want from it: [what this trip means to you — reconnecting with family history / a long-held goal / time with someone important / a personal challenge].
Practical constraints: [mobility / budget / travel companions / timeframe].
What I have already thought through: [anything you have already figured out].
What is stopping me: [the specific planning obstacles].

Please help me work out whether this is actually achievable, and if so, what the path from here to actually booking it looks like."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

That last instruction — what is the path from here to actually booking — is important. Planning and booking are different things. Many trips are planned but not booked. A plan that ends with a booking date and a committed deposit is a different thing from a plan that ends with a document.

[PAUSE]

Let me say one more thing about the sorting track before we close.

[PAUSE]

The things that end up in the "to sort" pile are almost always there for a reason. They are hard, or emotionally heavy, or they require decisions you have been avoiding, or they involve conversations that feel difficult.

[PAUSE]

AI does not make those things easy. It makes them more structured. It gives you a way to approach them that feels less overwhelming than a blank page.

[PAUSE]

But the courage to actually do them — to make the appointment, have the conversation, sign the document, book the trip — that still comes from you.

[PAUSE]

Your Cleared To Try for this track: this week, take one real step on the thing you have been sorting. Not a planning step. A doing step. Make the appointment. Make the call. Fill in the form. Book the thing.
