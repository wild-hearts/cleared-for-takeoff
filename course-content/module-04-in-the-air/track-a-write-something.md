# Module 4, Track A: Write Something

**Spoken duration:** ~25 minutes  
**Word count (spoken):** ~3,250 words  
**Format:** Direct to camera; on-screen examples of prompts and exchanges  
**On-screen text:** Prompt structures; worked examples  
**Tone:** Warm, permission-giving, specific — this is the writing track  

---

[LOOK TO CAMERA]

Welcome to the writing track.

[PAUSE]

If you chose this track, there is probably something you have been meaning to write for a long time. A story from your own life. A record of your family's history. A speech for someone you love. A collection of letters. A memoir — short or long, private or intended for others. Something you have been carrying and have not found the right way to put down.

[PAUSE]

AI will not write it for you. Let me be clear about that upfront, because it matters.

The experiences are yours. The memories are yours. The voice — the way you tell a story, the things you notice, the details that only you would think to include — that is irreplaceable and AI cannot replicate it.

[PAUSE]

What AI can do is help you get past the blank page. It can ask you the questions that draw the story out. It can help you organise material you have gathered. It can help you find the right words when you know what you want to say but cannot quite get there. It can edit what you have written so the meaning comes through more clearly. It can help you structure something that currently exists as fragments.

[PAUSE]

These are real and significant forms of help. They are the things that stop most people from finishing something they genuinely wanted to write.

[PAUSE]

This track covers five aspects of writing with AI:

Getting started when you do not know where to begin.
Drawing out the material — the stories and details.
Organising and structuring what you have.
Drafting and editing.
Working with specific formats: memoir, family history, eulogy, speech.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Part 1: Getting Started"]

The hardest part of writing is not the writing. It is beginning.

[PAUSE]

The most useful way to begin with AI is not to start writing. It is to start talking. Tell AI what you want to write, in the same way you would tell a friend — rough, exploratory, without worrying about whether it is clear or good.

[ON SCREEN: Getting started prompt]

"I want to write [what you want to write — a memoir / the story of my family / a collection of memories from my working life / a book about [topic]]. I am not sure where to start. Here is what I know I want:

[List anything you are certain of — the period you want to cover, the stories you know you want to include, the person or people at the centre of it, the feeling you want it to have].

Please help me work out how to approach this. Don't give me a finished plan — ask me questions first that will help you understand what I am trying to do."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

That last instruction — ask me questions first — is the key. The back-and-forth of being asked good questions and answering them often produces clarity about the project that you could not have reached alone.

[PAUSE]

You might spend thirty minutes answering AI's questions before you write a single word of the actual piece. That is not wasted time. That is some of the most productive work in the whole project.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Part 2: Drawing Out the Material"]

For personal writing — memoir, family history, stories from your life — the material already exists. It is in your memory, and in the memories of people around you. The challenge is drawing it out.

[PAUSE]

AI can help by asking you the kinds of questions a skilled interviewer asks.

[ON SCREEN: Memory drawing prompt]

"I want to write about [period of my life / specific event / particular person]. I have general memories but I want to go deeper. Please interview me about this — ask me questions that will help me remember specific details, sensory details, what people said, what I was thinking, what surprised me.

Start with: [the event, person, or period you want to explore]."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

The sensory detail instruction is important. Our most vivid memories attach to specific sensory experiences — the smell of a place, the sound of a voice, the feel of a particular moment. Generic questions produce generic memories. Sensory questions unlock specific ones.

[PAUSE]

When AI asks you a question, type your answer as fully as you can. Do not edit yourself. Do not worry about grammar or structure. Write like you are talking. The material in your answers — however rough — is the raw writing. It can be shaped later.

[PAUSE]

After the interview, ask AI to: "Summarise the main themes and stories I mentioned. What stood out? What could be the centre of this piece?"

You will often be surprised by what it notices.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Part 3: Organising and Structuring"]

Once you have material — through interviews, notes, letters, photographs, diaries, family documents — the challenge is organisation.

[PAUSE]

There are several structural choices in writing and most people are not sure which one fits their material:

Chronological — telling the story in the order it happened.
Thematic — organising around themes or questions rather than time.
A single event — zooming in on one story and radiating outward from it.
Braided — following more than one storyline and weaving them together.

[ON SCREEN: Structure prompt]

"I have the following material: [describe what you have — rough notes, specific stories, a time period, recurring themes].

Can you suggest two or three ways I could organise this? For each one, describe the approach and tell me what it would be best suited to. I will then decide which feels right for my material."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

Once you choose a structure, ask AI to help you build an outline:

"Using the [chronological / thematic / other] approach, please draft a rough chapter or section outline for my material. Keep it high-level — just headings and a sentence about what each section would cover."

That outline becomes your working plan. You do not have to follow it rigidly — but having it means you always know where you are in the larger project.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Part 4: Drafting and Editing"]

This is where AI's help is most visible, and where it is most important to stay in the driver's seat.

[PAUSE]

The basic approach: write a rough draft of a section yourself — or dictate it, or describe it — and then ask AI to help you shape it.

[ON SCREEN: Editing prompt]

"Here is a rough draft of a section I am writing. Please:

1. Tell me what is working — what is clear, vivid, or interesting.
2. Tell me what needs work — where it is vague, where I have told rather than shown, where it loses momentum.
3. Suggest a revised version of any paragraph that needs it.

My aim for this piece is [the feeling or effect you want — intimate and personal / formal and dignified / warm and funny / quietly honest].

[Paste your draft]"

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

Read AI's suggestions carefully. Accept the ones that sound right. Reject the ones that do not sound like you. The test is always: does this sound like something I would say?

[PAUSE]

AI tends toward certain writing habits — more formal than necessary, occasionally over-elaborate, sometimes too smooth. Your job is to keep the draft rough enough to be human, and let AI's input improve the clarity and structure without polishing out the voice.

[PAUSE]

If a section of your draft is very rough — half-sentences, notes to yourself, fragments — you can still ask AI to work with it:

"The following is rough — more notes than draft. Please help me turn this into proper prose while keeping my meaning intact. [Paste rough section]."

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Part 5: Specific Formats"]

Different writing forms have specific conventions. Let me cover four that are most common in this track.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Memoir"]

Memoir is personal narrative — true stories from your own life. It does not have to cover your whole life. The strongest memoirs are often about a specific period, relationship, or experience.

For memoir, the most important prompt you will use is the memory-drawing one from Part 2. Get the material first. The structure follows from what you have.

[PAUSE]

One common challenge in memoir: people include too much scene-setting and not enough scene. They describe where they were and how old they were and what year it was, but they do not drop the reader into a moment and let them experience it.

Ask AI: "I tend to over-explain rather than drop the reader into the moment. Here is a passage I have written. Can you show me a version that starts in the middle of the action instead?"

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Family History"]

Family history involves a combination of personal memory, family sources (letters, photographs, documents), and historical context.

AI can help you with the historical context piece. "I am writing about my father's experience as a migrant arriving in Australia in the early 1960s. What was the social and economic context in Australia at that time?" That background, properly contextualised, enriches the personal narrative.

For family history, interviews with family members are invaluable. AI can help you prepare interview questions: "I want to interview my 80-year-old aunt about her childhood in [place]. Please give me a list of questions that will draw out specific memories and sensory details."

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Eulogy"]

A eulogy is a particular challenge: you are writing under emotional pressure, to a deadline, for an audience, about someone you loved.

AI can take a significant amount of the pressure out of the drafting.

[ON SCREEN: Eulogy prompt]

"I need to write a eulogy for [name], who was my [relationship]. The service is in [timeframe].

Here is what I want to communicate about them: [key qualities, stories, what they meant to you].

Please draft a eulogy of approximately [4 to 6] minutes — around 500 to 700 words. The tone should be [warm and personal / dignified and formal / a balance of celebration and grief]. I will read it and change anything that does not sound like me or does not reflect them properly."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

Read the draft slowly. Change any phrase that would not come out of your mouth. Add the specific details — the name they used for a particular thing, the story only your family knows, the exact phrase they used to say. Those personal specifics are what makes a eulogy feel true.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Speech"]

Wedding speeches, 21st birthday toasts, retirement tributes, prize night addresses, farewell messages.

For speeches, tone and length are everything. Most people write too long and too formal.

[ON SCREEN: Speech prompt]

"I need to write a speech for [occasion — wedding, retirement, 21st birthday]. I am [my relationship to the person]. The speech should be about [length — two to three minutes / five minutes].

About the person: [three to five key things — their qualities, your relationship, a story or two].

Tone: [warm and funny / heartfelt without being heavy / formal but personal].

Please draft the speech, and then give me a note on any section that could be personalised further with a specific story or detail."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

That last instruction — where can it be personalised further — is where the speech becomes yours. The AI draft is a competent skeleton. The specific stories and details are the life.

[PAUSE]

Before we close this track: whatever you are writing, the most important thing is not to wait until it is ready. Nothing in writing is ever ready. It becomes ready by being written, and revised, and written again.

[PAUSE]

Start today. Even rough. Even wrong. The material is already there.

[PAUSE]

Your Cleared To Try for this track: spend two hours this week on your writing project. Use AI for the part that feels hardest — starting, structuring, editing, or finding the words. Treat the two hours as a commitment, not an aspiration.
