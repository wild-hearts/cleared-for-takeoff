# Module 1, Lesson 5: Setting Up Your Account Properly

**Spoken duration:** ~10 minutes  
**Word count (spoken):** ~1,300 words  
**Format:** Direct to camera; reference to tool walkthroughs in the Tools Library  
**On-screen text:** Key setting names, clearly labelled  
**Note to production:** The exact click-path for each tool is in the Module 1 Tool Walkthroughs (separate recordings). This lesson covers the concepts and what to look for. Refer students to the Tools Library for the specific steps.

[LOOK TO CAMERA]

You have an account. Before you use it, I want you to do one thing.

[PAUSE]

Not ten things. One thing. And then a couple of smaller things that are worth five minutes of your time before you start.

[PAUSE]

The most important setting in any AI account is the one that controls whether your conversations are used to train the model.

Let me explain what that means and why it matters.

[PAUSE]

When AI companies are building and improving their models, they look at conversations. Real conversations, between real people and the AI. They use those conversations to understand what works, what doesn't, where the AI is helpful, where it gets confused. This is how the AI gets better over time.

On most platforms, on the free tier, this is turned on by default. Which means: unless you change it, your conversations may be read by people at the company as part of this process.

[PAUSE]

This is legal. It's disclosed in the terms of service. It doesn't mean anyone is personally reading your grocery list with malicious intent. But it does mean that what you type could be seen by another person, as part of an improvement process.

[PAUSE]

The setting that turns this off is usually called something like: "Improve the model for everyone," or "Use conversation data for training," or "Data controls." The exact name varies by platform.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Where to find it — check the Tools Library for your specific platform"]

For the exact steps on each tool — where to click, what menu to look in, what the toggle looks like — I've recorded specific walkthroughs in the Tools Library. The Module 1 walkthroughs cover this for ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini and Copilot.

Come back to those after this lesson.

[PAUSE]

What I want you to understand here is what you're turning off and why.

When you disable this setting:
Your conversations are not used to train the model.
The AI still works exactly the same way for you.
The only thing that changes is that your data stays between you and the platform, not flowing into a training process.

[PAUSE]

For most of what you'll use AI for — drafting emails, summarising documents, planning things — this setting being on or off makes no practical difference to your experience. But if you ever use AI for something more personal — thinking through a health situation, drafting a difficult letter, working through a family matter — it's a reasonable thing to want turned off.

Turn it off once. It stays off.

[PAUSE]

Good. That's the important one.

[PAUSE]

Two smaller things that are worth doing while you're in your settings.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "1. Conversation history"]

First: conversation history.

Most platforms save your past conversations in a sidebar or a history list. This is genuinely useful — you can come back to something you were working on, pick up where you left off, refer back to something the AI said.

I'd suggest leaving this on. The benefit outweighs the minor privacy consideration for most tasks.

If you're ever having a conversation you don't want saved — something personal that you'd rather not have sitting in your history — most platforms let you start a "temporary" or "incognito" conversation. It runs normally but doesn't get saved when you close it.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "2. Account security"]

Second: account security.

Choose a password you don't use elsewhere. This is not AI-specific advice — this applies to every account you create online. If the same password protects your email, your bank, your Medicare, and your new AI account, a breach of any one of them puts all of them at risk.

Most platforms also offer two-factor authentication — a code sent to your phone that you need in addition to your password when you log in. If you're offered this: turn it on. It takes thirty seconds to set up and significantly increases the security of your account.

[PAUSE]

That's it. Three things total.

Turn off the training data setting. Decide what to do about conversation history. Set a strong password and turn on two-factor if it's offered.

[PAUSE]

I want to acknowledge something before we move on.

Going into settings on an unfamiliar platform can feel disorienting. There are often a lot of options, some of them with names that aren't immediately clear. If you open settings and you're not sure what you're looking at, use the Tools Library walkthroughs — they show exactly what to look for on each platform, with the current interface.

These walkthroughs get updated when interfaces change. The core settings we've covered in this lesson have been stable across all four platforms for a long time, but the exact location shifts occasionally. When in doubt, use the walkthrough.

[PAUSE]

One more thing before the next lesson.

You may notice, when you go into settings, that there are options related to "custom instructions" or "memory" or "personalisation." These are features that let you tell the AI things about yourself that it will remember across all conversations.

Don't worry about those right now. They're genuinely useful, and we'll cover them properly later in the course. For Module 1, you only need the three things I've mentioned. Everything else can wait.

[PAUSE]

Your account is set up. Your privacy settings are in order. In the next lesson — the last lesson in this module — you're going to look around the interface and say your first hello.

[PAUSE]

See you there.
