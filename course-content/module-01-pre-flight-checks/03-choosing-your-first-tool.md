# Module 1, Lesson 3: Choosing Your First Tool

**Spoken duration:** ~11 minutes  
**Word count (spoken):** ~1,500 words  
**Format:** Direct to camera; brief on-screen show of each tool's homepage  
**On-screen text:** Tool names and one-line descriptors  

---

[LOOK TO CAMERA]

The most common question I get asked about AI is not "how do I use it" or "what's it good for."

It's: "Which one should I use?"

[PAUSE]

And I completely understand why. There are four main options sitting in front of you — ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot — and if you've never used any of them, that list might as well be a menu in a language you don't speak.

[PAUSE]

Here's what I want to say at the start of this lesson:

The choice matters. But it matters a lot less than you think. The most important decision you can make right now is to pick one and start. Not to pick the perfect one.

[PAUSE]

That said — let me tell you what each one actually is, so you can make an informed choice rather than a random one.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: ChatGPT homepage, briefly]

First: ChatGPT.

ChatGPT is made by a company called OpenAI, based in the United States. It was the first AI tool to really break through into public awareness — the one that started the conversation, so to speak. Most people who have "tried AI" have tried ChatGPT.

It has a free version, which gives you access to a capable model with some usage limits. It has a paid version, currently around twenty Australian dollars a month, which gives you access to a more powerful model and removes most limits.

[PAUSE]

It's a good starting point. The interface is simple. There is more written about how to use ChatGPT than any other tool, which means if you get stuck, help is easy to find. The free version is genuinely functional — not a trial with everything useful locked behind a paywall.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: Claude homepage, briefly]

Second: Claude.

Claude is made by a company called Anthropic. It's the tool I use most in my own work.

If ChatGPT is the confident, fast-talking first option — Claude tends to be the more careful, more considered one. It's particularly good at writing tasks. It's good at long, nuanced conversations. It tends to say "I'm not sure" when it's not sure, rather than producing a confident wrong answer — which I find more useful than false certainty.

It also has a free version and a paid version at a similar price point to ChatGPT.

[PAUSE]

If you write — anything, professionally or personally — Claude is worth trying. If you want to have longer, more thoughtful conversations with an AI, Claude handles that well.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: Gemini homepage, briefly]

Third: Gemini.

Gemini is Google's AI tool. If you use Gmail, Google Docs, Google Drive, or Google Calendar — Gemini is going to increasingly be woven into all of those things. It's designed to work within the Google ecosystem.

If most of your digital life already lives in Google — your email, your documents, your calendar — Gemini is worth considering because it can eventually connect to those things and help you work with them directly.

If you're not already a Google Workspace person, Gemini is not particularly compelling compared to the first two options.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: Microsoft Copilot homepage, briefly]

Fourth: Copilot.

Copilot is Microsoft's AI tool, built into Microsoft 365 — which means if you use Outlook, Word, Excel, or Teams, Copilot is either already available to you or coming soon.

For organisations that run on Microsoft products, Copilot will become increasingly relevant. For individuals who primarily use Microsoft Office at home, it's worth knowing about.

The standalone Copilot app on the web is also free to use, without a Microsoft subscription.

[PAUSE]

So those are the four. Let me give you the honest summary.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: Simple two-column comparison — "Good if you..." for each tool]

ChatGPT: Good if you want the most established option, the most documentation, and a solid free tier with no particular ecosystem requirement.

Claude: Good if writing is central to what you want to do with AI, or if you want more careful, considered responses. Good if you'll be having long conversations.

Gemini: Good if you're already deep in Google Workspace and want AI that eventually connects to your existing files and email.

Copilot: Good if you're already running Microsoft 365 and want AI that plugs into your existing tools.

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

If you read that list and you still don't know which one to pick — I'm going to make it simple.

Start with ChatGPT or Claude. Both are free. Both work. Both are worth your time. Flip a coin if you need to.

[PAUSE]

Here's the thing nobody tells you: you are not getting married to a tool. You can try ChatGPT this week, try Claude next week, and decide you prefer one over the other. You can use both. Many people do. Different tools for different tasks is a completely legitimate approach.

This course is deliberately tools-agnostic. We don't teach ChatGPT. We teach a way of thinking and working with AI that applies across all of them. The skills transfer. What you learn with one tool works with all the others.

[PAUSE]

There is one practical note before we wrap this lesson up.

Each of these tools has a free tier and a paid tier. For this course, the free tier of any of them is enough. You do not need to pay for anything to complete Modules 1 and 2. When you get further into the course and start using AI for real work, you may find the paid tier worth considering — but that is a decision to make later, not now.

[PAUSE]

Your job at the end of this lesson is simple: pick one. Any one. If you are completely undecided: open chatgpt.com or claude.ai. Choose whichever looks less intimidating. We'll be using it in Lesson 4.

[PAUSE]

Next lesson: how to sign up safely. What to share. What not to share. And the one thing you should never type into a chat window.
