# Module 1, Lesson 2: Three Things AI Does Well, Three Things It Does Badly

**Spoken duration:** ~10 minutes  
**Word count (spoken):** ~1,380 words  
**Format:** Direct to camera; optional split-screen showing example outputs  
**On-screen text:** Six labelled items, revealed one at a time  

---

[LOOK TO CAMERA]

The fastest way to get frustrated with AI is to walk in expecting the wrong thing.

[PAUSE]

I have seen people give up on it because it confidently told them something incorrect. I have seen people decide it's useless because they asked it for today's stock prices and it got them wrong. I have seen people frustrated that it "doesn't remember" a conversation from last week.

All of those frustrations are real. And all of them make complete sense — if you don't know what you're actually dealing with.

[PAUSE]

Once you know what AI is good at and what it is genuinely bad at, you stop asking it for the wrong things. And you start getting a lot more out of it.

[PAUSE]

Three things it does well. Three things it does badly. Let's go.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "What AI does well — 1. Drafting and editing text"]

One. Drafting and editing text.

This is where AI genuinely earns its place. You have a letter you need to write and you've been putting it off for three weeks. You have an email to your real estate agent that you don't know how to phrase. You need to make a formal complaint about a bill and you're not sure of the right tone.

Give AI the situation. Tell it what you need to say and who you're saying it to. It will produce a draft in seconds. Not a perfect draft. A starting point that is significantly better than a blank page.

[PAUSE]

The same goes for editing. Paste in something you've written and ask it to tighten it, or make it more formal, or simplify it, or make it warmer. It is exceptionally good at adjusting tone. It will not change what you mean. It will change how it lands.

[PAUSE]

I write for a living. I use AI this way constantly. Not to replace my writing — to get unstuck when I'm staring at something that isn't working. It is the most useful thing I own that costs less than twenty dollars a month.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "What AI does well — 2. Summarising long things"]

Two. Summarising long things.

You have received a document you don't have time to read. A council planning notice. An insurance policy update. A rental agreement. A newsletter you've been meaning to get through. A report your accountant sent.

You can paste the text of that document into an AI chat window and ask it: "What are the main points in this? What do I actually need to know? Are there any deadlines or action items?"

It will tell you. In plain English. In thirty seconds.

[PAUSE]

This alone, for many people, is worth the price of the course. The relief of not having to read a twenty-page document cover to cover to find out whether anything in it matters to you is not a small thing.

Important note: this works for text you paste in. Not for things the AI doesn't have in front of it. Paste the text. Don't just describe the document.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "What AI does well — 3. Explaining how things work"]

Three. Explaining how things work.

You want to understand something — how superannuation drawdowns work, what the difference is between a fixed and variable rate mortgage, how Medicare levy surcharge is calculated, what a power of attorney actually does.

AI is very good at explaining things in plain English. Ask it to explain something to you "as if I have no background knowledge, in simple terms." It will produce a clear, readable explanation.

[PAUSE]

Important caveat: this is not legal advice. This is not financial advice. This is not medical advice. AI explaining how something works is genuinely useful for building basic understanding. It is not a substitute for talking to a professional when something matters. The course covers this in detail in Module 5. For now: good for understanding. Not a replacement for a lawyer or a doctor.

[PAUSE]

That's the three things it does well. Now the three things it does badly — and these matter just as much.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "What AI does badly — 1. Current, factual information"]

One. Current, factual information.

As we covered in the last lesson: AI is not live. It has a training cutoff. It does not know what happened last week.

Ask it who won last month's election and it may give you a confident, plausible, entirely wrong answer. Ask it for current interest rates, today's fuel prices, the current status of a court case, who currently holds a particular position — and it may get it completely wrong, with complete confidence.

[PAUSE]

The rule: anything that involves a current date, a current event, a current price, or a current status — verify it from a live source. Use the AI for context and background. Use Google or a current website for the actual facts.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "What AI does badly — 2. Maths"]

Two. Maths.

This surprises people. AI can look like it's doing maths very confidently. It can produce equations, work through calculations, present results with decimal places. And it can also be completely wrong.

AI is a language model. It predicts text. It was trained to produce text that looks like correct maths, not to actually perform calculations the way a calculator does.

For simple arithmetic, it is often fine. For anything involving multiple steps, percentages, compound calculations, or precise figures that matter — do not trust it without checking.

[PAUSE]

If you need to calculate something important — GST, superannuation, a budget breakdown — use a spreadsheet or a calculator. Use AI to help you understand the calculation, not to perform it.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "What AI does badly — 3. Knowing things about you"]

Three. Knowing things about you.

AI has no context about your life unless you give it that context, in the conversation, right now.

It does not know your name. It does not know your situation. It does not know your financial position, your health history, your family structure, your postcode. Every conversation is a blank slate.

This catches people out when they ask something like "what's the best superannuation fund for someone in my situation?" — and the AI produces a helpful-sounding but completely generic answer, because it has no idea what your situation is.

[PAUSE]

The solution is to give it context. Tell it your situation at the start. "I'm 58, I live in regional NSW, I'm self-employed, my partner works part-time, and I'm trying to understand..." That context changes everything about the quality of the response.

More on how to do this well in Module 2.

[PAUSE]

So. Three things AI does well: drafting text, summarising long documents, explaining how things work.

Three things it does badly: current factual information, maths, and knowing anything about you without being told.

[PAUSE]

Keep this in mind. Not because you need to memorise it — but because when something doesn't work the way you expected, it usually comes back to one of these six things.

[PAUSE]

Next lesson: how to choose your first tool. Which one should you use? And does it actually matter?
