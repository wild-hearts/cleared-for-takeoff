# Module 1, Lesson 4: Signing Up Safely

**Spoken duration:** ~10 minutes  
**Word count (spoken):** ~1,350 words  
**Format:** Direct to camera; brief on-screen text for key rules  
**On-screen text:** "The Postcard Rule" and three key never-share categories  

---

[LOOK TO CAMERA]

Before you create an account, I want to spend a few minutes on something that matters a great deal to this audience and that most AI courses skip entirely.

What are you actually handing over when you sign up?

[PAUSE]

This is not a paranoid question. It is a sensible question. And it has a sensible answer — which I'm going to give you now.

[PAUSE]

Let's start with what you're actually asked for when you sign up.

All four tools we talked about in the last lesson will ask you for the same two things: an email address, and a password.

Some of them will also ask you to verify your identity with a phone number — not because they need your number for anything suspicious, but because it's a standard security step that helps confirm you're a real person and not an automated account.

ChatGPT and Claude both let you sign up with just an email. You don't need to hand over your phone number unless you want to.

That's it. Email address. Password. Optionally, phone for verification. That's the sign-up.

[PAUSE]

Now. What you're not asked for — but what some people type in anyway — is where the risk lives.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "The Postcard Rule"]

I want to give you one rule that covers everything, and it's called the Postcard Rule.

A postcard goes through multiple hands before it reaches you. The postman, the sorting facility, possibly someone at your door. Anyone who handles it can read it. You wouldn't write your bank account number on a postcard. You wouldn't write your Medicare number. You wouldn't write your password.

Treat a chat window the same way.

[PAUSE]

Not because AI companies are nefarious. They're not. But because chat conversations go places — they may be reviewed by staff for safety, they are stored on servers, and depending on your privacy settings, they may be used to train future versions of the model.

None of that is a crisis. But it is a reason to apply the postcard rule.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Never type these into a chat window"]

Three categories of information you should never type into an AI chat.

[PAUSE]

One: Financial details.

Bank account numbers. Credit card numbers. BSB numbers. Your tax file number. Your superannuation account number. Even your approximate financial position in detail — "I have X in savings and X in super" is more than any AI tool needs to help you with a task.

If you're using AI to help you understand a financial document, paste the relevant text from the document. Do not type in your own financial details.

[PAUSE]

Two: Government identity information.

Medicare number. Passport number. Driver's licence number. Centrelink reference number. Date of birth combined with full name and address.

Any combination of information that someone could use to impersonate you is information that stays off the chat window.

[PAUSE]

Three: Other people's private information.

This one catches people by surprise. If you want AI to help you draft a letter about a dispute with someone, or write a message to a family member, or summarise a conversation you had with your doctor — you don't need to include full names, identifying details, or anything that would identify the other person without their knowledge.

Use placeholders. "My neighbour" instead of their full name and address. "A family member" instead of naming them. The AI doesn't need identifying details to help you with the task.

[PAUSE]

[BACK TO DIRECT CAMERA]

Now — and I want to be clear about this — the vast majority of what you will use AI for in this course involves none of these things.

Drafting an email. Summarising a document. Planning a trip. Understanding how something works. Writing a letter. None of that requires financial information, government IDs, or other people's private details.

The postcard rule kicks in for the exceptions. Apply it then, and you're fine.

[PAUSE]

There's one more thing worth mentioning before you sign up, and it's about the free tier.

When you use a free account, the default on most platforms is that your conversations may be used to improve the model. This means people at the company could theoretically review conversations — not to target you personally, but as part of the process of making the AI better.

On the paid tier, this is usually turned off by default or easily disabled.

This is not a reason to pay immediately. It is a reason to know it exists, and to be appropriately thoughtful about what you put in a free account.

[PAUSE]

In the next lesson, I'll show you how to turn off conversation training — which you can do even on the free tier of most tools. It's one setting, takes thirty seconds, and is the most important thing to do before your first real conversation.

[PAUSE]

For now: email address, password, phone if asked. The postcard rule for anything else. No financial details, no government IDs, no other people's identifying information.

[PAUSE]

That's lesson four. Go ahead and create your account — we'll configure it properly in the next lesson.
