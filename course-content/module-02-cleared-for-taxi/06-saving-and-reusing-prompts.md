# Module 2, Lesson 6: Saving and Reusing Prompts That Work

**Spoken duration:** ~10 minutes  
**Word count (spoken):** ~1,350 words  
**Format:** Direct to camera; brief on-screen example of a simple prompt library document  
**On-screen text:** Example prompt library layout; Cleared To Try exercise at end  

---

[LOOK TO CAMERA]

When you write a prompt that works — one that gets you a genuinely useful response — that prompt has value.

[PAUSE]

It might have taken you three attempts to get it right. You found the right context, the right task, the right format, the right tone. The response was exactly what you needed. And then you closed the chat and moved on.

Next week you need the same thing. And you start from scratch.

[PAUSE]

This lesson is about not doing that.

[PAUSE]

A prompt library sounds more technical than it is. It's a document — a Notes file, a Word document, a Google Doc, anything you can type into — where you keep prompts that worked well.

That's it. A list of useful prompts, with enough context so you remember what they were for.

[PAUSE]

Let me tell you why this is worth doing.

The first time you draft a formal complaint letter using AI, you'll need to work out the right way to ask. By the third time, you'll have a template that produces a good starting point every time you open the chat window.

The first time you ask AI to summarise a long document, you'll probably get something too long. You'll add a note: "Keep it to under 200 words, in plain English, with any action items listed at the end." That instruction is gold. Keep it.

[PAUSE]

Good prompts are tools. And tools are worth putting somewhere you can find them again.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: Simple example prompt library — plain text document format]

Here's what a basic prompt library looks like. Not a spreadsheet. Not a complex system. Just a document.

At the top: a heading. "Prompts I use regularly."

Then entries like this:

---

DRAFT A FORMAL LETTER OF COMPLAINT
Use when: writing to a body, company or government agency about a problem.
Prompt: "I need to write a formal letter of complaint to [organisation] about [problem]. Here's the background: [brief summary of situation]. I have [attempted to resolve it by doing X / not contacted them yet]. I want the letter to be firm and clear, but professional and not aggressive. Draft this as a formal letter of no more than 300 words, ready to send."

---

SUMMARISE A DOCUMENT
Use when: you've received something long that you need to understand quickly.
Prompt: "I'm going to paste a document below. Please summarise it in plain English: what is it about, what are the main points, and are there any deadlines or actions I need to take? Keep the summary to under 200 words." [Paste document here]

---

PREPARE FOR A DIFFICULT CONVERSATION
Use when: you need to have a conversation you're dreading and want to think it through first.
Prompt: "I need to have a difficult conversation with [person/role] about [topic]. My goal is [what outcome I want]. My concern is [what I'm worried about]. Can you help me think through how to approach this — what to say, what to avoid, and how to handle it if they react badly?"

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

Three entries. You might have thirty over time, or ten, or five. The number doesn't matter. What matters is that when you have a task that you know AI can help with, you have a starting point you trust rather than a blank screen.

[PAUSE]

A few things worth knowing about building this over time.

[PAUSE]

Don't save every prompt. Save the ones that apply to tasks you do regularly, or that took real effort to get right. A one-off prompt for a specific situation usually isn't worth saving. A prompt for a type of task you do monthly — absolutely worth it.

[PAUSE]

Write a note about what the prompt is for. Not just the prompt text. "Draft a formal complaint letter" is useful. Without the label, you'll open your document in six months and not remember what half the prompts do.

[PAUSE]

Update the prompts when you improve them. The first version of your complaint letter prompt might produce results that are good but slightly too formal. You add a line: "Avoid overly legalistic language." Update your saved prompt. Now it's better.

[PAUSE]

Think about your recurring tasks. Monthly tasks, seasonal tasks, things you dread doing. These are your highest-value targets for a prompt library.

Your annual tax summary. Your quarterly business activity statement letters. The update email you send every month. The minutes you take at a community meeting. The reference requests you occasionally have to write. Each of these can become a reusable starting point rather than a task you approach from scratch.

[PAUSE]

One more note before the Cleared To Try exercise.

Some people prefer to save prompts in their AI tool itself, using features like "custom instructions" or "memory" or a pinned conversation. These are valid approaches. The reason I recommend a simple external document is: it's portable. Your prompt library works across all four tools we covered. It doesn't live inside any one platform. If you switch tools, your library comes with you.

A simple document in your notes app or your cloud drive is all you need.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Cleared To Try — Module 2"]

Your Cleared To Try exercise for this module.

Pick one task you do every week. Something that currently takes more time than it should. An email you write. A document you have to deal with. A plan you have to put together.

Use AI to help you with that task this week. Apply what you've learned in this module: give it context, tell it the task, specify the format, name the tone.

When you're done: note how long it took compared to doing it without AI. Write down the prompt that worked, if it did.

That's the exercise. One real task. One real week. One note about what you noticed.
