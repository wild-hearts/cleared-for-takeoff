# Module 2, Lesson 4: When to Be Specific, When to Be Vague, When to Start Over

**Spoken duration:** ~12 minutes  
**Word count (spoken):** ~1,580 words  
**Format:** Direct to camera; on-screen text for the three modes; brief example of what "start over" looks like  
**On-screen text:** Three scenarios labelled: Specific / Open / Start fresh  

---

[LOOK TO CAMERA]

There is a version of the AI frustration cycle that goes like this.

You ask a question. The response is not quite right. You add more detail. The response is still not quite right. You add more detail again. Now you've sent six messages and the AI is trying to satisfy six different sets of instructions at once, and the response has become a mess.

[PAUSE]

This lesson is about recognising when that is happening — and knowing which of three modes will actually help.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Mode 1: Be specific"]

Mode one: be specific.

This is the mode most people know they need, and underuse anyway.

Being specific is the right approach when you have precise requirements. When the output needs to match a particular format, audience, length, or set of constraints.

[PAUSE]

Examples of tasks that benefit from specificity:

A letter that needs to be in formal legal style, under 400 words, addressed to a named party.

A budget spreadsheet broken down by specific categories you name.

A travel plan that covers specific dates, specific cities, a specific daily budget, specific mobility requirements.

A message to a specific person — your GP, your landlord, your body corporate — where tone and relationship matter.

[PAUSE]

For tasks like these, the more precisely you describe what you want, the better the result. Use the four-part framework from Lesson 2. Name the constraints. Be explicit about what matters.

The investment in writing a thorough prompt up front saves you three rounds of back-and-forth trying to get it right.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Mode 2: Be deliberately open"]

Mode two: be deliberately open.

There are tasks where specificity is the wrong approach. Where you don't yet know what you want, and you need the AI to help you figure that out.

[PAUSE]

Brainstorming. Exploring. Getting ideas. Figuring out what questions you should be asking.

These tasks benefit from open prompts.

[PAUSE]

"I'm thinking about starting something small — a side project, maybe some kind of service, maybe a product. I have no firm ideas yet. What questions should I be asking myself?"

"I want to do something different for my mother's birthday this year. She's 80 and we usually do the same restaurant dinner. What are some alternatives we haven't thought of?"

"I'm trying to understand why I keep getting frustrated with a particular situation at work. I haven't figured out what the real problem is. Can you help me think through it?"

[PAUSE]

These prompts are deliberately underdetermined. You're not asking the AI to produce a specific output. You're asking it to help you think. And AI is actually very good at this — at asking useful questions, at offering angles you haven't considered, at structuring something that currently feels like a cloud.

[PAUSE]

The risk with open prompts is that you get something too generic. If that happens, don't add more specificity — narrow the question. "I don't mean something that requires a large investment. Just something I could test without spending more than a few hundred dollars."

Open, then narrowing. That's the rhythm for exploratory conversations.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Mode 3: Start fresh"]

Mode three: start fresh.

This one is underused, and it's often the fastest solution.

[PAUSE]

When a conversation has gone sideways — when you've added so many corrections and clarifications that the AI is clearly confused about what you actually want — the answer is not to add another correction. The answer is to close the conversation and start a new one.

[PAUSE]

A new conversation is a fresh slate. No accumulated confusion. No competing instructions. No weird tangent from three messages back that's still influencing the response.

This feels wasteful. It isn't. Starting a new conversation with a clearer, better-structured prompt — one that incorporates what you learned from the failed attempt — is almost always faster than trying to rescue a conversation that's gone off course.

[PAUSE]

There's a related situation worth naming: the conversation that started fine but has grown so long that the AI is losing track of the early details.

Most AI tools can hold a certain amount of conversation in mind at once. In a very long conversation, the earliest messages gradually become less influential. You might find the AI seems to have forgotten something it agreed to three pages back.

When this happens: start fresh. Paste in the most important context from the previous conversation, then continue from there.

[PAUSE]

How do you know which mode you're in?

[PAUSE]

If you have clear requirements: specific.
If you're figuring out what you want: open.
If the conversation is going in circles or producing muddled results: start fresh.

[PAUSE]

One more thing about iteration, because it connects all three of these.

Iteration — refining within a conversation — is normal and expected. It is not a sign that you're doing something wrong. The first response is almost never the final response. It's the starting point of a dialogue.

The sign that iteration is working: each response gets closer to what you need.

The sign that iteration has stopped working: each response introduces new problems or ignores the corrections you've already made.

When iteration stops working — start fresh.

[PAUSE]

A quick note on the regenerate button, because it's easy to overlook.

Most platforms have a button near the AI's last response that says "Regenerate," or shows a refresh icon. Clicking this asks the AI to try the same prompt again, producing a different response.

This is useful when the AI's approach is wrong but your prompt was right. Maybe the tone was off. Maybe it went in an unexpected direction. Regenerating gives you a fresh attempt without changing anything you wrote.

[PAUSE]

Specific when you know what you want. Open when you're figuring it out. Fresh start when things have gone sideways.

[PAUSE]
