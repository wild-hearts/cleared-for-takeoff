# Module 2, Lesson 3: How to Ask AI to Ask You Better Questions

**Spoken duration:** ~12 minutes  
**Word count (spoken):** ~1,600 words  
**Format:** Direct to camera; on-screen text for the three key phrases; one worked example with visible exchange  
**On-screen text:** The three "reverse prompting" phrases; example conversation flow  

---

[LOOK TO CAMERA]

Here is a technique that most people never discover, and that makes a significant difference when the task is complicated.

[PAUSE]

Instead of giving AI the information and asking for a response — ask AI what it needs to know, before you give it anything.

[PAUSE]

Let me explain why this is useful.

When you have a complex question — something medical, something legal, something financial, something personal that involves a lot of context — you often don't know which parts of your situation are relevant and which aren't. You don't know whether the AI needs to know your age, your state, your income, your history, your relationship, your deadline.

So you either dump everything in — which is overwhelming — or you leave out the important details by accident — which produces a generic answer.

[PAUSE]

There is a third option. You ask AI to interview you.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: Three phrases to use]

Three phrases that activate this:

"Before you answer, what information would help you give me a better response?"

"What would you need to know about my situation to help me with this?"

"What are you assuming about me right now, and are those assumptions right?"

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

Let me show you what this looks like in a real situation.

You want to ask AI about managing your superannuation as you approach retirement. This is a topic where context matters enormously. Your age, your current balance, whether you're still working, your partner's situation, whether you have a self-managed fund — all of it changes the advice.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: Example exchange — displayed as a chat]

You type: "I want to understand my options for accessing my superannuation as I get closer to retirement. Before you explain anything, can you tell me what you'd want to know about my situation to give me relevant information rather than generic information?"

[PAUSE]

The AI responds with a list of questions. Something like:

"To give you relevant information rather than general guidance, it would help to know:

How old are you, and are you still working?
What type of super fund do you have — industry, retail, or self-managed?
Are you planning to retire fully or move to part-time first?
Do you have a partner whose super or income would be part of the picture?
Are you thinking about this in terms of years or months?

You don't need to answer all of these — even a few will help me give you something more useful."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

Notice what just happened. The AI has identified the variables that matter. Now you can answer the ones that are relevant to you, skip the ones that aren't, and get a response that's actually tailored to your situation — without having to know in advance which details mattered.

[PAUSE]

This technique is particularly valuable in four situations.

[PAUSE]

First: health questions.

If you want to ask AI about a symptom, a medication, a diagnosis, or a treatment option — context is everything. Age, existing conditions, other medications, how long the symptom has been present. Before you describe the whole thing, ask AI what it needs. It will ask the right questions. You answer them. Then it gives you something genuinely useful.

Important note, which you'll hear again in Module 5: AI information about health matters is a starting point for understanding, not a substitute for your doctor. What this technique helps you do is arrive at your doctor's appointment better informed, with better questions.

[PAUSE]

Second: legal and financial situations.

"I'm in a dispute with a neighbour about a fence" means very different things depending on your state, whether you own or rent, what the council bylaws say, how long you've been in the property. Ask AI what it needs. You'll get information that's relevant to your actual situation, not to the average fence dispute.

[PAUSE]

Third: decisions with a lot of moving parts.

"Should I renovate or sell?" depends on so many factors that a generic answer is nearly useless. Ask AI what it would need to know to help you think through this. It will structure your own thinking as well as its response.

[PAUSE]

Fourth: writing tasks where the audience is important.

Before asking AI to draft something, ask: "What would you need to know about who I'm writing to, and why, to get the tone right?" It will ask about the relationship, the situation, the goal, the history. Your answers make the draft significantly better.

[PAUSE]

There is a simpler version of this technique that is worth knowing as well.

After AI gives you an answer that feels too generic — you can ask:

"What are you assuming about my situation that might not be true?"

[PAUSE]

This surfaces the hidden assumptions in the response. The AI was assuming you're in a particular state. Assuming you have a particular type of fund. Assuming a particular relationship to the problem. When it names those assumptions, you can correct the ones that are wrong, and the next response will be considerably more useful.

[PAUSE]

I want to acknowledge something here.

Some people feel strange about this. It can feel like you're over-complicating what should be a simple exchange. "Why can't I just ask the question?"

You can. For simple questions, just ask. This technique is for when the situation is genuinely complex and you want a response that's genuinely relevant rather than a response that covers all eventualities and therefore addresses none of yours properly.

[PAUSE]

The return on this investment is high. Spending one extra exchange — one message asking what the AI needs — can save you three or four rounds of getting generic answers and trying to explain why they don't quite apply to you.

[PAUSE]

Try it once, on something that has a lot of moving parts. Notice the difference in the quality of the response.

[PAUSE]

Next lesson: knowing when a prompt needs more detail, when to leave things deliberately open, and when the right call is to stop and start fresh.
