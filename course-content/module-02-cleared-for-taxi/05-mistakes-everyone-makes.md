# Module 2, Lesson 5: Mistakes Everyone Makes in Week One

**Spoken duration:** ~11 minutes  
**Word count (spoken):** ~1,500 words  
**Format:** Direct to camera; on-screen labels for each mistake  
**On-screen text:** Each mistake named, with "what to do instead" in smaller text beneath  
**Tone:** Warm, direct, matter-of-fact — not a lecture, a debriefing  

---

[LOOK TO CAMERA]

I want to walk you through the mistakes that almost everyone makes when they start using AI — not to make you feel bad about making them, but so you can recognise them quickly and know what to do.

[PAUSE]

Every one of these is recoverable. None of them are failures. They're just patterns that slow you down, and they're a lot easier to spot when someone's named them for you.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Mistake 1: Treating it like Google"]

Mistake one: typing keywords instead of having a conversation.

We covered this in Lesson 1, but it's worth naming again here because it's the most common and the most persistent habit. After years of keyword-searching, reverting to it under pressure is almost automatic.

When you notice a response feels too generic, or misses the point — check your prompt. Was it a request or a keyword string? Add the context, the task, the format. The result will change.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Mistake 2: Accepting the first response without reading it"]

Mistake two: treating the first response as final.

AI produces the first plausible response, not necessarily the best one. The first response is a draft.

Read it. Identify what's right and what's not. Then continue the conversation: "This is mostly good but the tone is too formal" or "Can you add something about X?" or "The third paragraph doesn't match what I asked for."

The AI is not offended by feedback. It doesn't have a stake in its first response. It will adjust.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Mistake 3: Asking for current facts and trusting the answer"]

Mistake three: trusting AI for current, real-time information.

Asking AI what the current pension rate is. What the RBA cash rate is right now. Who currently holds a government position. What the current rules are for a particular scheme.

AI has a training cutoff. It may give you a confident answer that was correct eighteen months ago and is now out of date. Or it may have the right number but the wrong year.

For current information: use a live source. A government website. A recent news article. The institution directly. Use AI to help you understand what you're looking at — not to be your primary source of current facts.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Mistake 4: Asking too many things at once"]

Mistake four: loading one prompt with multiple unrelated tasks.

"Can you write me a letter to my landlord about the hot water, and also help me plan a trip to Cairns, and also explain how franking credits work?"

The AI will attempt all three. None of them will be as good as they would have been if you'd asked one at a time. It's not a failure of AI — it's a structuring problem.

One task per conversation is a useful habit, especially while you're getting started. When you've finished one task, start a fresh conversation for the next one.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Mistake 5: Giving up when the first attempt doesn't work"]

Mistake five: deciding AI is useless because of one bad result.

This is the most consequential mistake on this list, because it stops people from getting to the point where they actually benefit.

Bad first results are normal. They mean your prompt needed more context, or the task was more complex than a single message could capture, or you hit one of the limitations we've covered. They don't mean AI isn't useful for that task.

When a result is wrong or unhelpful: tell the AI what was wrong. Add the missing context. Try a different approach. Or start fresh with a better prompt. Almost always, one of those three moves gets you somewhere useful.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Mistake 6: Forgetting it has no memory"]

Mistake six: expecting AI to remember the last conversation.

You had a detailed conversation about planning a renovation. You got some good ideas. You come back the next day and continue from where you left off.

The AI has no idea what you're talking about. Every new conversation is a blank slate.

The fix is simple: at the start of a new conversation, give a brief summary of the context. "Last week I was planning a kitchen renovation in a 1970s house in regional Victoria, budget of around $40,000. I'm picking that back up. Here's where I got to..." Thirty seconds of recapping. Then continue.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Mistake 7: Not giving it enough credit when it gets it right"]

Mistake seven: using AI for one thing and never exploring further.

People discover AI is good at drafting emails. They use it for drafting emails. Full stop.

Which is fine, and genuinely valuable. But AI is good at a surprising number of things that most people don't think to try until someone mentions it.

Summarising. Planning. Explaining. Researching. Writing. Editing. Brainstorming. Comparing options. Understanding documents. Preparing for difficult conversations.

You will discover most of these through Module 3 of this course, which is designed specifically to expand what you reach for AI for. But in the meantime — when a task feels tedious, or a document feels daunting, or a conversation feels hard to prepare for — it's worth asking: "Could AI help with this?"

The answer will surprise you more often than not.

[PAUSE]

Seven mistakes. All common. All fixable.

[PAUSE]

If there is one overarching principle from this list, it's this: the quality of what you get from AI is almost always a direct reflection of the quality of what you give it. Vague prompts, generic results. Rich context, specific results. Feedback given, improved results.

[PAUSE]

AI is not magic, and it is not useless. It's a skill. And skills get better with use.

[PAUSE]

Last lesson in this module: how to keep what works — saving and reusing prompts so you're not starting from scratch every time.
