# Module 2, Lesson 2: The Four-Line Prompt

**Spoken duration:** ~12 minutes  
**Word count (spoken):** ~1,650 words  
**Format:** Direct to camera; on-screen text for each of the four elements; two full worked examples  
**On-screen text:** The four elements displayed individually, then assembled into a complete prompt  

---

[LOOK TO CAMERA]

In the last lesson, we talked about giving AI more than keywords — giving it a conversation.

This lesson gives you a structure to do that without having to think too hard. Not a rigid template. A checklist of four things that, when they're all in your prompt, consistently produce better results.

[PAUSE]

I use these four things every day. I don't think about them consciously anymore — they've become habit. But when something isn't working, I come back to this list and I can almost always identify what's missing.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "The four parts of a good prompt"]

Context. Task. Format. Tone.

Four words. Let me walk through each one.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "1. Context — Who you are and why you're asking"]

One: Context.

This is the background the AI needs to understand your situation. Who you are, what your circumstances are, what has led to this moment.

Context is what stops AI from giving you generic advice when you need specific advice.

[PAUSE]

Without context: "How do I dispute a bill?"

With context: "I'm a pensioner in regional South Australia. I've received a power bill that's three times higher than my usual amount. The increase started in July. I've called the provider twice but got no resolution."

Same question. Completely different starting point for the AI.

[PAUSE]

Context doesn't need to be long. A sentence or two is usually enough. The question to ask yourself: "What would I tell a friend before asking them this question?" That's your context.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "2. Task — What you want the AI to do"]

Two: Task.

This is what you actually want. Not the situation — the action.

Draft a letter. Explain how this works. Summarise this document. Give me three options. Write a script for a phone call. List the steps. Help me think through this decision.

[PAUSE]

Task is where people most often go vague. "Help me with my electricity bill" is a context-and-topic, not a task. "Draft a formal complaint letter to my electricity provider about a billing dispute" is a task.

Be specific about what you want the AI to do. It can do many things — it needs to know which one.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "3. Format — How you want the answer"]

Three: Format.

How do you want the response presented?

A short paragraph. A bulleted list. A formal letter. A casual message. A step-by-step numbered process. A table comparing options. Two sentences.

[PAUSE]

Format is the element most people forget to include — and it's the one that produces the most immediate improvement when you add it.

Without a format request, AI will produce whatever it thinks is appropriate. That's often a medium-length paragraphed response. Sometimes that's exactly right. Often you wanted something much shorter, or much more structured, or in a completely different shape.

[PAUSE]

Tell it how you want the answer delivered, and you get it in a form you can actually use.

"Give me this as a bullet list."

"Draft it as a formal letter, ready to sign."

"Keep your answer to two paragraphs."

"I want three options, each described in one sentence."

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "4. Tone — How it should sound"]

Four: Tone.

Formal or casual. Friendly or firm. Direct or gentle. Professional or personal.

Tone matters especially when AI is writing something on your behalf — a letter, an email, a message. The AI has no idea whether you're writing to your local member of parliament or to your neighbour. Unless you tell it.

[PAUSE]

"Formal and professional."

"Warm but clear — like a message to a friend."

"Firm, not aggressive. Assertive without being rude."

"Simple enough for someone without a legal background."

[PAUSE]

One sentence about tone. That's enough.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: The four parts listed together — Context / Task / Format / Tone]

So. Context, Task, Format, Tone. Let me put this together with a full example.

[PAUSE]

Scenario: You want help drafting a message to your council about a pothole that's been reported twice and still hasn't been fixed.

[PAUSE]

Without the framework: "Write something to council about a pothole."

With the framework:

[ON SCREEN: Full assembled prompt]

"I'm a resident in [suburb], ACT. There's a significant pothole on [street name] near [cross street] that I've reported twice through the council website — once in March and once in May. No repair has been done and it's getting worse. I want to write a firm but polite email to the council asking for an update and a commitment to a repair timeline. Please draft this as a formal email, professional in tone, around 150 words."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

That prompt contains all four elements. Context: resident, location, history of reporting. Task: draft a firm polite email asking for update and timeline. Format: formal email, around 150 words. Tone: professional.

The AI will produce a draft you can send with minimal editing.

[PAUSE]

Let me give you one more example, different in character.

Scenario: You want help planning a 70th birthday dinner for your partner. You have no idea where to start.

[ON SCREEN: Full assembled prompt]

"My partner turns 70 in two months. We live in Newcastle, NSW. I'd like to organise a dinner for about 20 people — close family and a few old friends. We don't want anything too formal or fussy, but it should feel special. Budget is around $2,000 all in. I'd like some options for how to structure this — venue ideas, whether to do a set menu or buffet, that sort of thing. Give me three different approaches I could take, each described in a short paragraph."

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

Context: partner's milestone birthday, Newcastle, 20 guests, character of the occasion, budget. Task: three different approaches. Format: three short paragraphs. Tone is implicit in "not too formal or fussy, but should feel special" — that's a tone instruction embedded in the context.

[PAUSE]

You'll notice the four parts don't need to be in order. They don't need to be labelled. They don't need to be in separate sentences. They just need to all be there. The AI will find them.

[PAUSE]

Now — I want to be honest about something.

Not every prompt needs all four. If you're asking a simple question — "what's the difference between a fixed rate and variable rate mortgage?" — you don't need to set up Context, Task, Format and Tone. Just ask.

The framework is for the tasks where it matters. The letter you need to write. The plan you need to develop. The document you want summarised in a particular way. The email that needs to hit a specific tone.

[PAUSE]

When something doesn't work, come back to the four elements. Which one is missing? Almost always, that's the problem.

[PAUSE]

Next lesson: what to do when you're not sure what you want — and how asking AI the right question first can save you a lot of back and forth.
