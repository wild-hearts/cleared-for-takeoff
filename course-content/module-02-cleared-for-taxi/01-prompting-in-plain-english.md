# Module 2, Lesson 1: Prompting in Plain English

**Spoken duration:** ~12 minutes  
**Word count (spoken):** ~1,650 words  
**Format:** Direct to camera with two on-screen prompt comparisons  
**On-screen text:** Side-by-side examples, contrasting keyword prompts with conversational ones  

---

[LOOK TO CAMERA]

There is one habit that almost everyone brings to AI that produces consistently mediocre results. And it comes from twenty years of using Google.

[PAUSE]

When we search for something online, we have learned to type fragments. Keywords. The minimum amount of information needed to surface a result. "Best dentist Canberra." "Flights Melbourne." "How to remove red wine stain."

We do this because search engines reward it. A search engine is looking for web pages that contain your keywords. The more specific your keywords, the better the results. So we trained ourselves to strip out the context, the reason, the detail — and just give the engine what it needs.

[PAUSE]

AI is not a search engine.

[PAUSE]

This is the single most important thing I want you to take from this lesson. AI is not a search engine. It is a conversation partner. And conversation partners need context.

[PAUSE]

Let me show you what this looks like in practice.

[ON SCREEN: Split screen — LEFT: "flights canberra melbourne july" | RIGHT: "I need to fly from Canberra to Melbourne on a weekend in July — somewhere around the 12th. I'm travelling alone, my budget is around $300 return, and I'm flexible on times. Can you help me understand what to look for and which airlines typically cover this route?"]

On the left: what most people type the first time they try this.

On the right: what actually gets useful results.

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

The left prompt gets you something generic. A list of airlines, maybe. Some general advice about booking in advance.

The right prompt gets you a response that is actually tuned to your situation. It knows you're flying alone. It knows your budget. It knows you're flexible. It can factor all of that in.

[PAUSE]

The difference between those two prompts is not intelligence. It's the same information you already have in your head — you just have to transfer it to the chat window.

[PAUSE]

Let me give you another example, closer to everyday life.

[ON SCREEN: Split screen — LEFT: "letter body corporate noise complaint" | RIGHT: "I need to write a letter to my body corporate about a noise problem with a neighbour. The noise is mostly late at night on weekends — loud music and gatherings. I've mentioned it to the neighbour twice and nothing has changed. I want the letter to be firm but not aggressive. It should prompt the body corporate to take action without making me look difficult. Can you draft something I can send?"]

On the left: six words that tell the AI almost nothing.

On the right: a full picture of the situation, the history, and what outcome you actually want.

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

Look at what the right prompt contains. It has the situation. It has context — this has been going on, you've tried to address it yourself. It has a specific goal — prompt action without looking difficult. And it has a format request — a draft letter.

The AI cannot invent those details. It can only work with what you give it. If you give it fragments, it fills in the gaps with guesses. Sometimes the guesses are fine. Often they're not quite right.

[PAUSE]

The good news is that typing a proper request instead of keywords takes about thirty extra seconds. That's the entire investment.

[PAUSE]

I want to address something that comes up here.

Some people feel strange about typing full sentences to a computer. It feels formal, or slightly embarrassing, or like they're writing a letter to a machine. That feeling goes away fast. Within a week of using AI regularly, writing conversationally to it feels completely normal. But even if it never stops feeling slightly odd — the results are worth it.

[PAUSE]

There is also a version of this problem that goes in the other direction. Some people, especially people who are more detail-oriented, put so much information into a prompt that the AI can't identify what they actually want from it.

Eight paragraphs of context. Every relevant detail. The entire history of the situation. And then, buried at the end: "what should I do?"

[PAUSE]

If you notice you are doing this — and you'll know because the AI's response seems to miss the point — we'll cover what to do about it in Lesson 4.

For now: the target is somewhere between "keywords" and "an essay." Enough context to be specific. Clear about what you want. Written the way you'd explain it to a helpful person on the phone.

[PAUSE]

Let me give you a simple formula for right now. Not the full framework — that's next lesson. Just a starting point.

Before you type your prompt, ask yourself three questions:

What is my situation?
What do I want the AI to do?
What does a good answer look like?

Answer those three things in your prompt, in plain English, in whatever order feels natural, and you will be ahead of most people who use AI regularly.

[PAUSE]

You don't need to be formal. You don't need to use technical language. You don't need to say "please" or "thank you" — though there's nothing wrong with it if that's how you naturally write. You don't need to worry about grammar. You don't need to spell everything correctly.

The AI understands natural, imperfect, conversational language. That's what it was trained on.

[PAUSE]

One last thing before we move on.

When a prompt doesn't get you what you wanted — and this will happen — the answer is almost never to give up and decide AI isn't useful. The answer is to give it more context. Tell it what you were hoping for. Tell it what's missing from the response it gave you.

"That's helpful, but it's too formal for what I need. Can you rewrite it in a more casual tone?"

"This is close, but I need it to be shorter — around three paragraphs."

"The letter is good but it doesn't mention that I've already spoken to the neighbour twice. Can you add that?"

This is iteration. Every conversation with AI is an ongoing dialogue, not a one-shot attempt.

[PAUSE]

Stop typing keywords. Start having a conversation.

Next lesson: a four-part framework that makes every prompt better — and that you will use without thinking about it within a week.
