# Module 5, Lesson 3: Scam Awareness — How AI Is Being Used to Deceive

**Spoken duration:** ~12 minutes  
**Word count (spoken):** ~1,580 words  
**Format:** Direct to camera; on-screen examples  
**On-screen text:** Scam types; red flags; what to do  
**Tone:** Direct, clear, not alarmist — gives the tools to recognise and respond  

---

[LOOK TO CAMERA]

This lesson is about something that deserves plain speaking.

[PAUSE]

AI has made it significantly easier to create convincing scams. Voices that sound like family members. Messages that read like they are from institutions you trust. Images and videos of real people saying things they never said. Financial scams that previously required a sophisticated criminal operation and now require very little.

[PAUSE]

I am not telling you this to frighten you. I am telling you this because the best defence against a sophisticated scam is knowing what it looks like before you encounter it.

[PAUSE]

This lesson covers four types of AI-enabled scams that are currently operating in Australia, and a clear framework for what to do if you think you are being targeted.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Scam Type 1: Voice Cloning and the Grandparent Scam"]

Voice cloning is technology that can replicate a person's voice from a short audio sample — sometimes as little as thirty seconds of existing audio. That cloned voice can then be used to make phone calls that sound, to someone who loves them, exactly like that person.

[PAUSE]

The Grandparent Scam works like this.

You receive a phone call. It is your grandchild's voice — or your child's, or your partner's. They are upset. They are in trouble. They have been in a car accident, or arrested, or are in hospital. They need money urgently and they are begging you not to tell anyone — because they are embarrassed, or because telling someone will make the legal situation worse.

A second person comes on the line — a policeman, a lawyer, a hospital administrator. They explain the process. They tell you money needs to be transferred, or gift cards purchased, or cash provided to a courier who will collect it.

[PAUSE]

The voice sounds real. The urgency is real. The love you feel for that person is real.

The call is not.

[PAUSE]

If you receive a call like this:

Hang up. Immediately.
Call the person directly on their actual number — the number you already have for them, not a number provided in the call.
If you cannot reach them, call another family member who can check on them.

Do not transfer money. Do not purchase gift cards. Do not give anything to a courier. Do not call a number provided to you in the call.

[PAUSE]

The rule is simple: any call that creates extreme urgency and asks you to act before telling anyone else — is a scam. A real emergency does not require secrecy. Real institutions do not ask for gift cards.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Scam Type 2: Messages Pretending to Be Institutions"]

AI can now write convincing formal English in any style. This means that the previously reliable tell of a scam email — poor grammar, strange phrasing, obvious spelling errors — no longer applies.

[PAUSE]

Scam messages from supposed government agencies, banks, Medicare, the ATO, myGov, or toll operators are now written in fluent, professional English. They look like the real thing. They have logos, official-sounding language, and genuine urgency.

[PAUSE]

What to look for:

The sender address or contact number. Hover over the sender address in an email — does it actually come from the organisation it claims to be? Scam emails often use addresses that look similar but are not genuine: "ato-support@gov-australia-ato.com" rather than a real government address.

The request. Legitimate government agencies and banks do not ask you to provide personal information by clicking a link in an email. They do not demand immediate payment by a method you have never used before. They do not threaten arrest if you do not call back immediately.

If in doubt: do not click the link. Do not call the number in the message. Go directly to the official website you already know, or call the institution's published number.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Scam Type 3: Deepfakes — Videos and Images of Real People"]

A deepfake is a video or image that has been artificially generated or manipulated to show a real person doing or saying something they never did or said.

[PAUSE]

In a scam context, this most commonly means: a video of a well-known person — a politician, a celebrity, a business leader — appearing to endorse a financial product, investment scheme, or get-rich-quick opportunity.

[PAUSE]

The video looks real. The person looks real. What they are saying sounds plausible. They may be shown in a familiar setting — a news interview, a social media post, a television segment.

[PAUSE]

Warning signs:

Watch the mouth closely. Deepfakes often have subtle inconsistencies in lip sync — the mouth movements do not quite match the words.
Check whether the person's eyes blink naturally. Unnatural blinking patterns are a common deepfake tell.
Look at the edges of the face and hair. Where the face meets the background, deepfakes often show subtle distortion.
Ask yourself: would this person actually say this? A senior government minister endorsing a cryptocurrency investment on social media is implausible. That implausibility is itself a signal.

[PAUSE]

More importantly: if you see a video that encourages you to invest money in something — regardless of who appears to be endorsing it — do your research independently. Contact a financial adviser or Scamwatch before you give anyone any money.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Scam Type 4: Romance and Relationship Scams"]

Romance scams — where a fraudulent persona builds an emotional relationship with someone over time and eventually requests money — are not new. But AI has made them significantly more sophisticated.

[PAUSE]

The scammer can now maintain a consistent, detailed, believable persona over extended conversations. The messages are warm, intelligent, well-written. They remember what you told them. They ask thoughtful follow-up questions. The relationship feels real.

[PAUSE]

Eventually, the request comes. Usually after weeks or months. Usually framed as an emergency — medical costs, a business problem, a crisis that only a transfer of money can resolve.

[PAUSE]

The rule here is the same one that has always applied: a person you have never met in person, who asks for money, is operating a scam. Regardless of how much you feel you know them. Regardless of how convincing the conversation has been. Regardless of how compelling the emergency seems.

Meeting in person should be non-negotiable before any money changes hands — and a legitimate person will understand this.

If someone you have only met online asks for money: stop. Tell someone you trust. Contact Scamwatch.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "What to do if you think you have been scammed"]

If you believe you have been the target of a scam — whether or not you transferred money:

Call your bank immediately if money was transferred. Banks have dedicated scam teams and can sometimes reverse transactions if they are contacted quickly.

Report to Scamwatch. That is scamwatch.gov.au — it is run by the Australian Competition and Consumer Commission. Reporting helps protect others.

If you transferred money via gift cards: contact the gift card company directly.

Do not be ashamed. Scams are designed by intelligent, experienced people to exploit entirely normal human emotions: love for family, trust in institutions, fear of legal consequences, longing for connection. Being targeted is not a character failing.

[PAUSE]

One last thing. The most effective defence against scams is not suspicion. It is the habit of pausing before you act.

[PAUSE]

Any communication that creates urgency, asks for money or personal information, requests secrecy, or feels designed to prevent you from checking with someone else — deserves a pause.

A real emergency can usually wait twenty minutes while you call someone you trust.
A real institution can be called back on a number you find yourself.
A real relationship can withstand you asking a question before you send money.

[PAUSE]

Pause. Check. Then decide.
