# Module 5, Lesson 5: What Never to Put Into an AI Tool

**Spoken duration:** ~10 minutes  
**Word count (spoken):** ~1,350 words  
**Format:** Direct to camera; on-screen list  
**On-screen text:** The categories; the workaround approach  
**Tone:** Clear, direct, practical — not frightening  

---

[LOOK TO CAMERA]

This lesson is a short one, and it covers something simple.

[PAUSE]

There are specific categories of information that should not go into an AI chat window. Not because AI tools are unsafe in general, but because these particular types of information carry risk that is disproportionate to any benefit you would get from sharing them.

[PAUSE]

I will be direct about what they are, and I will give you a practical workaround for each category so you can still get the help you need.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Category 1: Financial account details"]

Your bank account numbers. Credit card numbers. BSB codes. Your bank's online password or PIN. Your investment account numbers.

[PAUSE]

You do not need to share these to get AI help with financial questions. AI can help you understand how a system works, draft correspondence to your bank, or plan a budget — none of which requires your actual account numbers.

The workaround: refer to your accounts in general terms. "My bank" rather than naming the account. "A credit card" rather than the card number. Describe the situation without including the identifying financial details.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Category 2: Government identity numbers"]

Your Tax File Number. Your Medicare number. Your Centrelink Customer Reference Number. Your passport number. Your driver's licence number.

[PAUSE]

These are the numbers that identify you to government systems. If they are compromised — if they end up somewhere they should not be — the consequences can include identity theft, fraudulent tax returns, and the very difficult process of reclaiming your identity with official records.

The workaround: describe your situation without including the numbers. "I have a Centrelink payment arrangement" rather than your CRN. "My most recent tax return" rather than your TFN. AI does not need your specific identifier to help you understand the system or draft a response.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Category 3: Passwords and security credentials"]

Your password for any account. The answers to your security questions. One-time codes sent to your phone.

[PAUSE]

There is no legitimate AI task that requires you to share a password. If anyone — AI or human — asks for your password, treat it as a warning sign. Real tools and real institutions do not need your password to help you.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Category 4: Detailed medical and mental health information with your name attached"]

This one requires a nuance.

[PAUSE]

AI can be very helpful for understanding health information, preparing for appointments, making sense of a diagnosis. We covered this in Module 3. None of that requires you to share your full name alongside detailed medical information.

[PAUSE]

The risk is not that you ask about a medication or a condition. The risk is that you create a detailed, identifiable profile — your name, your diagnosis, your medications, your medical history — in a single AI conversation that is then stored.

The workaround: ask about conditions, medications, and processes without attaching your full identifying information. "I have been prescribed [medication]" does not require your name. "I am trying to understand [condition]" does not require your date of birth.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Category 5: Other people's personal information"]

Information about other people is their information to share, not yours.

[PAUSE]

If you are asking AI for help with a situation involving a family member, a friend, a neighbour, a colleague — describe the situation in terms of your experience and what you need help with, rather than building a detailed profile of the other person.

"I am having a difficult relationship with a family member who has [general situation]" is different from sharing another person's full name, address, medical history, or personal circumstances.

[PAUSE]

The practical guidance here is: share what you need to share to get useful help, and no more. Most of the time, you need much less detail than you think you do to get a good response.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Category 6: Anything sent to you under confidentiality"]

If you have received documents, correspondence, or information under a confidentiality agreement — a legal settlement, a workplace agreement, a non-disclosure arrangement — you may have legal obligations about how that information is handled.

[PAUSE]

Pasting confidential documents into an AI tool would likely constitute sharing them with a third party, which may breach those obligations. If you are uncertain, check with a solicitor before pasting any document that came with a confidentiality condition.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "The practical test"]

Here is a simple test you can apply to any piece of information before you include it in an AI prompt.

[PAUSE]

Ask yourself: if this conversation were stored and later accessed by someone I did not intend — would this piece of information cause me harm?

If the answer is yes — leave it out. Describe the situation in more general terms instead.

If the answer is no — it is probably fine to include.

[PAUSE]

Most of the time, the answer is no. Most of the tasks we have covered in this course involve information that is either generic (how does this system work) or only moderately personal (my situation is roughly like this). Neither of those categories poses meaningful risk.

The categories I have listed here — financial accounts, government IDs, passwords, detailed health profiles with identifying information, others' personal data, confidential documents — are the exceptions. They require more care. Now you know what they are.

[PAUSE]

One more thought before we move on.

[PAUSE]

Following these guidelines is not burdensome. It does not significantly limit what you can do with AI. It just changes the level of detail you include, and it does so in a way that almost never affects the quality of the help you get.

[PAUSE]

"My bank account ends in 4432" versus "my bank account" — the latter works just as well for almost everything you would want to ask.

Keep the identifying specifics out. Get the help you came for.
