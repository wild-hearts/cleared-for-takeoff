# Module 5, Lesson 1: Hallucinations — What They Are and How to Recognise Them

**Spoken duration:** ~11 minutes  
**Word count (spoken):** ~1,480 words  
**Format:** Direct to camera; on-screen examples  
**On-screen text:** Definition; example hallucinations; recognition checklist  
**Tone:** Clear, calm, matter-of-fact — not alarmist  

---

[LOOK TO CAMERA]

There is a word you will have seen in articles about AI. Hallucination.

[PAUSE]

It is a strange word for what it describes. In everyday language, a hallucination is something you see that does not exist — a perceptual experience with no basis in reality.

In AI, a hallucination is something similar. It is when an AI tool produces information that seems real, is stated confidently, and is completely wrong.

[PAUSE]

Not a mistake in the way a calculator makes a mistake, where the error is obvious. An AI hallucination looks and reads exactly like correct information. It is in the same format, the same tone, with the same confidence. The only difference is that it is wrong.

[PAUSE]

This is important to understand. So let me explain why it happens.

[PAUSE]

AI tools are trained on vast amounts of text. They learn patterns — which words tend to follow which other words, which kinds of answers follow which kinds of questions, what a credible-sounding response looks like in a given context.

When an AI generates a response, it is not retrieving a fact from a database and returning it. It is producing the text that, based on its training, seems like the most plausible continuation of what you have written.

[PAUSE]

Most of the time, this produces accurate information. The patterns AI has learned reflect the actual world — because the text it was trained on mostly describes the actual world.

But sometimes — particularly when the question asks about something obscure, specific, or outside the densest part of the training data — the AI produces text that sounds like an accurate answer but is not one. It fills the gap with something plausible rather than something true.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: Examples of hallucination types]

Let me give you the categories where hallucinations are most common.

[PAUSE]

The first is specific facts: names, dates, statistics, titles.

An AI might confidently cite a research study that does not exist. It might tell you a law was passed in 2019 when it was actually passed in 2014. It might give you a statistic — "studies show that 68% of people over 65..." — with a figure that has no basis in any real study.

These errors are particularly insidious because they are specific. A specific number, a specific date, a specific citation — these all feel like evidence of accuracy. They are not, necessarily.

[PAUSE]

The second category is professional and specialist information.

Legal information. Medical information. Financial information. Regulations, guidelines, eligibility rules.

In these areas, AI can be significantly useful for explanation and context — as we covered in Module 3. But specific details in these areas are where hallucinations can matter most. "The maximum claim under this policy is $15,000" — if that is wrong, the consequence is real.

[PAUSE]

The third category is quotes and references.

"As [well-known person] famously said..." — and the quote is fabricated.
"This is consistent with the findings of [researcher] in their 2018 paper..." — and the paper does not exist.

AI does not have a clear sense of the difference between things it knows and things it is generating. It does not flag uncertainty the way a careful person does. It produces the content that fits the pattern, which sometimes means generating a plausible-sounding citation for a source that was never written.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: How to recognise a hallucination]

How do you recognise a hallucination? The honest answer is: not always, not just by reading it. A hallucination looks exactly like correct information.

What you can do is develop habits that make you less likely to rely on hallucinated information in situations where it matters.

[PAUSE]

Habit one: ask yourself what kind of information this is.

Is it an explanation of how something works? That is where AI is most reliable.
Is it a specific fact — a date, a figure, a name, a citation? That is where you verify.

If the distinction is not clear, default to verifying the specific details.

[PAUSE]

Habit two: ask the AI to show its working.

"Can you tell me where this information comes from?"
"Is there a way for me to verify this?"
"Are you confident in this figure, or should I check it?"

A well-designed AI tool will acknowledge uncertainty when it is real. If it says "I am confident in this" — verify anyway. If it says "I am not certain, you should check" — it is being appropriately honest. Either way, verify the specific facts that matter.

[PAUSE]

Habit three: use a live source for anything that matters.

The ATO for tax information.
Services Australia for Centrelink and Medicare.
A government health website for medical facts.
A licensed professional for legal and financial specifics.

This is not a failure of AI. It is an appropriate division of labour. AI gives you context and understanding. Live and official sources give you specific, current, verifiable facts.

[PAUSE]

Habit four: be especially sceptical of specificity.

The more specific a claim — the more it involves a precise figure, a named person, a dated event — the more worth verifying it is. Specificity sounds like accuracy. It is not always.

[PAUSE]

Habit five: do not share AI-generated specific information with others without verifying it first.

If AI tells you something that you then pass on as fact — to a family member, a friend, in a letter to an organisation — and that information is wrong, the consequences extend beyond you. Verify before you repeat.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: What hallucinations don't mean]

Let me be clear about what hallucinations do not mean.

[PAUSE]

They do not mean AI is unreliable for everything. AI is genuinely reliable for explaining how things work, helping you understand concepts, drafting documents, summarising material, brainstorming ideas, and dozens of other tasks we have covered in this course. In these areas, the risk of hallucination causing real harm is very low.

They do not mean you should avoid using AI. They mean you should use it with appropriate awareness of where verification is and is not necessary.

They do not mean AI is uniquely dangerous. Books can contain wrong information. Newspapers print corrections. Well-meaning friends give incorrect advice. The difference is that AI sounds confident across a very broad range of topics, which makes the hallucination risk more consistent than it is with any single human source.

[PAUSE]

The appropriate response to the hallucination risk is not avoidance. It is the same response that good researchers have always had to any source of information: awareness of the source's strengths and limitations, and verification of specific facts in anything that matters.

[PAUSE]

AI is a very capable research assistant and writing tool who sometimes makes things up. Knowing that about a person would not make you stop working with them. It would make you check the facts before you relied on them.

[PAUSE]

That is all this lesson is asking you to do.
