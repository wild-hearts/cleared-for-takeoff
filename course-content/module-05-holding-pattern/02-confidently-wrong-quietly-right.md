# Module 5, Lesson 2: Confidently Wrong and Quietly Right

**Spoken duration:** ~11 minutes  
**Word count (spoken):** ~1,480 words  
**Format:** Direct to camera; on-screen examples  
**On-screen text:** Signals to watch for; verification triggers  
**Tone:** Clear, calm, develops judgement without creating anxiety  

---

[LOOK TO CAMERA]

In the last lesson, we talked about hallucinations — what they are and why they happen.

[PAUSE]

This lesson is about the adjacent problem, which is in some ways harder to navigate.

[PAUSE]

AI does not signal its own uncertainty reliably. It does not say "I am quite sure about this" in one response and "I am really just guessing here" in another. Both responses sound the same. Both are delivered in the same calm, fluent, organised way.

[PAUSE]

This means the reader has to develop their own sense of when to trust a response and when to question it. And that sense — that judgement — is a skill. It develops over time. This lesson is designed to accelerate it.

[PAUSE]

The skill has two sides. Knowing when AI is likely to be reliable. And knowing when it is likely to be wrong.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "When AI is likely to be reliable"]

Let's start with where AI is most trustworthy.

[PAUSE]

Explanations of how things work.

When you ask AI to explain a concept — how compound interest works, how a superannuation transition to retirement strategy functions, what the difference between a will and a codicil is — you are asking it to do something it is genuinely good at. Explaining processes and principles, in plain language, at the level you need.

The risk of error here is lower because the general shape of these explanations is well-represented in the training data, and errors in explanation tend to produce responses that sound off or inconsistent — which you are more likely to notice.

[PAUSE]

Drafting and writing.

When AI helps you draft a letter, plan a structure, brainstorm ideas, or write an explanation of something — it is generating language, not retrieving facts. The risk is not that it hallucinates the content of your complaint letter. The risk is that the draft is poorly worded, or the wrong tone, or not quite what you needed.

These are risks you can detect by reading the draft. They are recoverable.

[PAUSE]

General processes.

How to apply for something. How a system typically works. What steps are usually involved in a particular process. These tend to be reliable because the processes are well-documented in the training data and change slowly.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "When AI is likely to be unreliable"]

Now the other side.

[PAUSE]

Specific current figures.

Anything that involves a number that changes: tax thresholds, pension rates, interest rates, fund balances, fees, prices. These change regularly. AI's training is not updated in real time. A number that was accurate eighteen months ago may not be accurate today.

[PAUSE]

Specific names, citations, and references.

"Professor [Name] at the University of [Place] conducted a landmark study..." — if you did not prompt AI to use a specific source, any citation it produces should be verified before you rely on it.

This applies to quotes from well-known figures, references to specific legislation, and any named sources or authorities. They may be real. They may be fabricated. Verify before you cite.

[PAUSE]

Local, regional, and niche information.

AI is best-informed about things that are well-represented in its training data — which tends to mean things that are widely written about in English. Local government processes in a specific council area. A specific Australian industry regulation. A niche legal provision. A small local business or institution.

The less widely covered something is, the more cautious you should be.

[PAUSE]

Very recent events.

AI does not have access to what happened last week, or in most cases what happened in the past year or two. If you ask about something recent and AI answers confidently — that confidence is a red flag. It may be drawing on patterns from before the cutoff and presenting them as current.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Signals to watch for in a response"]

Here are specific signals in an AI response that should prompt you to verify before relying.

[PAUSE]

A specific statistic with no source.

"Studies show that 72% of..."
"Research indicates that..."
"According to experts..."

Where did this come from? If you did not direct AI to a specific source, it may have generated this figure. Ask: "Where does this statistic come from? Is there a specific study I can look at?" If the answer is vague or produces a reference you cannot verify, treat the statistic with caution.

[PAUSE]

An unusually confident response on a complex topic.

When a question has a genuinely uncertain or contested answer — something in medicine, law, finance, or a complex political situation — a response that is too clean and confident may be glossing over genuine uncertainty.

Good AI responses on complex topics will often say: "This varies depending on..." or "I would recommend checking with a professional for your specific situation." If those caveats are absent where they should be, the response may be oversimplifying.

[PAUSE]

Information you cannot find when you look for it.

If AI mentions a law, a case, a study, a report, a guideline, or a specific fact, and you cannot find it when you search for it — that is a strong signal of a hallucination. The absence of a verifiable source matters.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Building the habit of verification"]

Verification does not have to be slow or laborious. Here is a practical framework.

[PAUSE]

For understanding and explanation: no verification needed for routine use. If the explanation helps you understand a concept and you are using it as background knowledge, not as the basis for a significant decision, trust it provisionally.

[PAUSE]

For a specific fact that will inform a decision: verify. A quick search of the relevant government website, official source, or reliable publication takes two minutes and confirms whether the figure is current and accurate.

[PAUSE]

For something you are going to repeat, publish, or act on: verify. If you are going to pass on information to someone else — write it in a letter, post it publicly, act on it financially or legally — verify before you do.

[PAUSE]

For professional or medical situations: use AI for understanding and question preparation. Use a qualified professional for specific advice.

[PAUSE]

One technique worth building into your regular use: after a response on a topic that matters, ask AI to flag its own uncertainty.

"How confident are you in this information? Is there anything here I should verify from an official source?"

A good AI tool will identify what it is uncertain about. It will not always catch everything — but asking the question shifts you from passive recipient to active evaluator, which changes the quality of your engagement with the response.

[PAUSE]

The goal of this lesson is not to make you anxious about AI. It is to make you appropriately calibrated.

[PAUSE]

Appropriate calibration means: trusting the responses that are likely to be reliable, verifying the ones that might not be, and knowing the difference between the two.

[PAUSE]

That is the same skill good readers bring to any information source. AI is a new source, with its own particular strengths and failure modes. Now you know what they are.
