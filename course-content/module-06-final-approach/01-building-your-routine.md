# Module 6, Lesson 1: Building Your Routine

**Spoken duration:** ~10 minutes  
**Word count (spoken):** ~1,350 words  
**Format:** Direct to camera; on-screen examples  
**On-screen text:** The one-page prompt library concept; daily/weekly routine ideas  
**Tone:** Warm, practical, encouraging without being prescriptive  

---

[LOOK TO CAMERA]

The gap between people who genuinely benefit from AI and people who dabble with it and move on is not about intelligence, and it is not about technology confidence.

[PAUSE]

It is about routine.

[PAUSE]

People who benefit from AI regularly have found a way to make it part of how they already move through the day. They do not treat it as a special tool they get out for special occasions. They reach for it the way they reach for a phone or a calculator — habitually, without thinking about it, when the task is the right shape.

[PAUSE]

Building that kind of habit takes time and intention. This lesson is about how to build it deliberately.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "One tool"]

The first thing: commit to one tool for daily use.

[PAUSE]

You have been introduced to four tools in this course — ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and Copilot. You may have tried more than one. Pick one as your primary and use it consistently for the next three months.

[PAUSE]

Not because the others are worse. Because fluency comes from consistency. When you use the same tool regularly, you learn its particular qualities — how it handles certain kinds of requests, what it tends to do well, what it tends to do less well. That familiarity makes you faster, more effective, and better at getting what you need.

[PAUSE]

Switching tools constantly resets that learning. Pick one. Stay with it.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "One habit"]

The second thing: identify one specific, recurring task where AI will be your default starting point.

[PAUSE]

Not a list of tasks. One.

[PAUSE]

It might be the monthly letter you write to a tenant, a client, or a body corporate. The weekly summary email you send to a small group. The research you do when you are trying to understand a new product, service, or situation. The planning you do at the start of a project.

[PAUSE]

Pick the task that happens most regularly and where you know AI can help. For three months, make it your default to open AI first for that task, rather than starting from scratch.

[PAUSE]

After three months, add another. Then another. Growth by addition is much more sustainable than trying to overhaul everything at once.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "One page"]

The third thing: maintain a prompt library. One page.

[PAUSE]

We covered this in Module 2, Lesson 6. Now that you have completed the course and have a much broader sense of what AI can do, it is worth revisiting and building out your library.

[PAUSE]

Your prompt library should have at minimum:

The prompts you use for your recurring tasks.
The prompts from this course that felt most immediately useful.
Any prompt you have written that worked well and that you want to be able to use again.

[PAUSE]

The one-page format is a guide, not a rule. What matters is that the library is in a place you will actually look at it — your notes app, a pinned document in Google Drive, a saved note on your phone — and that you can find a prompt in thirty seconds or less.

[PAUSE]

The prompt library reduces friction. Friction is the enemy of habit. When you have a library you trust, opening AI and getting started takes less effort. Less effort means more consistent use.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "What a sustainable routine might look like"]

Let me give you a picture of what this looks like in practice — not as a prescription, but as an illustration.

[PAUSE]

Monday morning. There is an email to write that requires some care — a follow-up on a matter you have been pursuing. Instead of sitting with a blank email and thinking, you open AI, paste your prompt template for follow-up emails, fill in the specifics, get a draft, read it, adjust two things, and send it. Fifteen minutes instead of forty-five.

[PAUSE]

Wednesday. You receive a letter that uses language you are not sure about. You paste the relevant section into AI and ask for a plain-English explanation. You understand it in three minutes instead of reading it four times and still not being certain.

[PAUSE]

Friday. You are planning the coming week and you are dreading a conversation you need to have. You use AI to think through how to approach it — what to say, what to avoid, how to handle the likely pushback. You go into the weekend with more clarity and less anxiety.

[PAUSE]

None of those examples involve AI doing something extraordinary. They involve AI reducing friction on tasks that were already part of the week — tasks that took more time and more effort than they needed to.

[PAUSE]

That is what a sustainable routine looks like. Not a dramatic transformation. A series of small frictions removed.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "The prompt library revisited"]

One practical exercise before the next lesson: spend twenty minutes updating your prompt library.

[PAUSE]

Go through the course paths and tracks you completed in Modules 3 and 4. Look at the prompt structures we covered. Write down the three or four that are most relevant to your life.

Add them to your library, with a note on what each one is for.

If you have already used AI for something and it worked well — add that prompt to the library too, even in rough form.

[PAUSE]

Your library does not need to be perfect. It needs to be there. A rough list of six useful prompts in a note on your phone is worth more than a beautifully organised system that takes three months to build and never gets used.

[PAUSE]

Start rough. Refine over time. The library grows as your use grows.
