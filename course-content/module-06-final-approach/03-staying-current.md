# Module 6, Lesson 3: Staying Current — Following AI Without Panic

**Spoken duration:** ~10 minutes  
**Word count (spoken):** ~1,330 words  
**Format:** Direct to camera; on-screen resource list  
**On-screen text:** Recommended sources; what to tune out  
**Tone:** Calm, practical, reassuring — the goal is informed equanimity  

---

[LOOK TO CAMERA]

AI is moving fast. That is not a controversial thing to say. The tools that existed three years ago are significantly different from the ones that exist today, and three years from now they will be different again.

[PAUSE]

If you try to keep up with everything — every announcement, every update, every new model, every article about what AI will mean for the future — you will exhaust yourself. And most of that information will not change what you do.

[PAUSE]

This lesson is about a more sustainable approach: staying informed enough to be confident without following the news cycle in a way that creates anxiety.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "What you actually need to follow"]

Most of the AI news you will encounter falls into one of four categories.

[PAUSE]

Category one: updates to tools you already use.

This is the category worth paying attention to. When ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, or whichever tool you use regularly releases a significant update, it is useful to know what has changed and whether it affects how you use it. Most tools communicate significant updates through their apps or websites. Reading the release notes — usually a short summary — is enough.

[PAUSE]

Category two: new tools and capabilities.

This is worth a light awareness. When something genuinely significant appears — a new type of AI capability, a new tool that does something none of the existing ones do — it is worth knowing about. But most "new AI tool" announcements are variations on existing capabilities, often with significant hype and modest practical change.

A good filter: does this new thing help with something I currently want to do? If yes, explore it. If not, note it and move on.

[PAUSE]

Category three: safety and privacy developments.

This is worth staying informed on, because it affects how you use AI. Major policy changes — new Australian legislation, significant changes to the privacy policies of tools you use, significant revelations about how AI systems work — are worth reading when they come up.

But you do not need to track this daily. A monthly glance at a trusted source is enough.

[PAUSE]

Category four: predictions and speculation about what AI will mean.

This is the category to largely tune out.

[PAUSE]

Articles about how AI will transform every industry, eliminate most jobs, change human civilisation — these may or may not be directionally correct, but they are not actionable. You cannot use speculation about the future to change what you do today. And the volume and intensity of this kind of coverage is designed to generate attention, not to inform your decisions.

Read these occasionally, with scepticism. Do not let them drive your AI anxiety.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Sources worth following"]

A small set of sources is better than many.

[PAUSE]

For Australian context:

The ABC Technology section covers significant AI developments with an Australian lens and generally reasonable balance. Not daily reading, but worth checking when something major happens.

The Office of the Australian Information Commissioner — oaic.gov.au — publishes guidance on privacy and data, including AI-related guidance. Worth checking every few months.

The Australian Competition and Consumer Commission's Scamwatch — scamwatch.gov.au — publishes regular updates on new scam types, including AI-enabled ones. Worth a monthly check.

[PAUSE]

For general AI literacy:

The help sections and update logs of the tools you use. These are primary sources and the most practically useful.

MIT Technology Review — technologyreview.com — for longer-form, relatively careful coverage of AI developments. Monthly reading, not daily.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "What to tune out"]

There are specific types of content worth actively filtering.

[PAUSE]

YouTube channels and social media accounts built around "AI will change everything." These are primarily engagement-optimised content. The incentive is to keep you alarmed and watching, not to give you accurate information.

Articles that begin with "AI is going to..." or "By 2030, AI will..." These are speculation. Treat them as such.

Any source that produces multiple AI-panic stories per week. Real, significant developments happen infrequently. High-frequency alarm content is almost always amplified noise.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "The update rhythm"]

Here is a practical rhythm for staying current without being overwhelmed.

[PAUSE]

Weekly: open the AI tool you use most regularly and notice if anything has changed. Read the release notes if there is an update. Five minutes.

Monthly: check Scamwatch for new scam alerts. Glance at one trusted technology source for anything significant. Twenty minutes.

Quarterly: review your prompt library. Add prompts that have been useful. Remove ones you no longer use. Try one new prompt structure from this course that you have not explored yet. One hour.

Annually: consider whether the tool you are using is still the best fit for your needs. Explore one new AI capability that has emerged since the last year. Revisit one lesson from this course that you want to go deeper on. Half a day.

[PAUSE]

That rhythm — weekly, monthly, quarterly, annually — keeps you current without requiring you to treat AI news as a daily responsibility.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "The equanimity principle"]

One last thought.

[PAUSE]

The people who seem most anxious about AI tend to be people who follow the news cycle closely, read a lot of speculative content, and feel a responsibility to stay on top of everything.

[PAUSE]

The people who seem most confident and capable tend to be people who use AI regularly, pay attention to the tools they use, and largely ignore the noise.

[PAUSE]

Equanimity about AI does not come from understanding the technology at a deep level. It comes from having a clear, settled sense of what you use it for, what you trust it with, and what you do not. That settled sense is something you have now built.

[PAUSE]

You do not need to track everything. You need to use what you have, stay informed on the things that affect your use, and stay grounded when the coverage tries to unsettle you.

[PAUSE]

That is a sustainable relationship with a fast-moving technology. And it is well within reach.
