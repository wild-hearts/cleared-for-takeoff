# Module 6, Lesson 2: When Not to Use AI

**Spoken duration:** ~10 minutes  
**Word count (spoken):** ~1,320 words  
**Format:** Direct to camera; on-screen summary  
**On-screen text:** Categories where AI does not belong  
**Tone:** Clear, direct, dignified — this is not a warning, it is a mark of sophistication  

---

[LOOK TO CAMERA]

Knowing when not to use a tool is a mark of understanding it.

[PAUSE]

A hammer is a fine tool. Reaching for a hammer when you need a screwdriver is not. Knowing the difference is not a criticism of hammers. It is competence.

[PAUSE]

This lesson is about the situations where AI is not the right tool — not because it is dangerous, but because something else serves the purpose better.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "When human connection is the point"]

The first category: situations where the value is in the human connection itself.

[PAUSE]

If your friend has just lost someone they love, and you want to reach out — the impulse to use AI to draft the message is understandable. Writing feels hard under emotional pressure. But the message itself — however imperfect — is the act of caring. The fact that it comes from you, with your words, your history, your specific memory of that person — that is what the message is.

[PAUSE]

AI can help you find a structure. It can suggest an opening line when you have none. That kind of light support is different from using AI to generate a message you then send without substantially making it your own.

There is a distinction worth holding: AI as a starting point for your words, versus AI as a replacement for your words. In moments of genuine human significance, the distinction matters.

[PAUSE]

This applies to condolences, to meaningful celebrations, to apologies, to conversations where what you say has real weight to the person receiving it. Use your words. AI can help you find them, not write them for you.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "When you need a professional"]

The second category: situations that require qualified advice.

[PAUSE]

We have covered this throughout the course, but it is worth stating plainly.

AI can explain how superannuation works. A financial adviser can tell you what to do with yours.
AI can explain what a power of attorney is. A solicitor can draft one that is legally valid for your circumstances.
AI can explain what a medication does. Your GP and pharmacist can advise whether it is right for you.
AI can explain how the age pension is calculated. Centrelink can tell you what you are actually eligible for.

[PAUSE]

The difference is the word "your." AI works at the level of general understanding. Professionals work at the level of your specific situation, your specific needs, your specific legal and financial and medical circumstances.

[PAUSE]

For anything with significant consequences — legal, financial, medical, tax — use AI to prepare for the professional conversation, not to replace it.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "When the task requires recent information"]

The third category: anything that depends on current, real-time information.

[PAUSE]

Election results. Today's share prices. The current status of a bushfire or weather event. What a specific store currently stocks. Whether a current policy applies. Today's interest rate.

[PAUSE]

For anything where currency matters — where the information is only useful if it is accurate right now — use a live source. A news website, a government website, an official price-checking service, a live weather service.

AI cannot give you what it does not have. It can give you context, background, and historical information. It cannot give you today.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "When the task requires your judgment"]

The fourth category: decisions that are genuinely yours to make.

[PAUSE]

AI can help you think through a decision. It can present the considerations. It can play devil's advocate, give you counterarguments, help you see angles you had not considered.

But for significant decisions — particularly those involving values, relationships, and your own life direction — the decision belongs to you. Not because AI's analysis is untrustworthy, but because the act of making the decision is part of what the decision means.

[PAUSE]

Should I move closer to my family, or stay where I have built my life? Should I retire now, or wait another two years? Should I have this difficult conversation with my sibling, or let it go?

These are questions where AI can help you think. They are not questions where you want AI to tell you the answer. The answer has to come from the part of you that knows what matters.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "When trust is what is being built"]

The fifth category: learning situations where the struggle itself is the education.

[PAUSE]

If you are learning a language and you use AI to translate every sentence you are not sure about — you will translate correctly, but you will not learn. The uncertainty, the effort to recall, the minor failure and the correction — that is where learning happens.

If you are learning to cook and you ask AI to plan every menu rather than developing your own sense of what works together — your cooking improves less than if you made the judgment calls yourself.

There is a version of AI assistance that does the work for you, and a version that helps you do the work yourself. The second version produces more lasting capability.

[PAUSE]

This is not a reason to avoid AI in learning contexts. Module 4 covered how AI can be a genuinely valuable learning companion. It is a reason to notice the difference between AI helping you understand something and AI removing the need for you to understand it.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "The summary"]

Not to use AI when: the value is in the human connection.
Not to use AI when: you need specific professional advice for your situation.
Not to use AI when: the information needs to be current.
Not to use AI when: the decision is genuinely yours to make.
Not to use AI when: the effort itself is what produces the growth.

[BACK TO CAMERA]

[PAUSE]

Knowing these is not a limitation on what AI can do for you. It is clarity about what it cannot, and what belongs elsewhere.

[PAUSE]

People who understand this use AI more confidently, not less. They reach for it readily for the tasks it suits, and they do not reach for it for the tasks it does not. That clarity is what confident use looks like.
