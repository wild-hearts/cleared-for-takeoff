# Module 6, Lesson 5: Bringing Someone Along

**Spoken duration:** ~10 minutes  
**Word count (spoken):** ~1,320 words  
**Format:** Direct to camera  
**On-screen text:** The three types of people to bring along; how to introduce AI  
**Tone:** Warm, specific, practical — speaks to the reality of this audience's relationships  

---

[LOOK TO CAMERA]

You have learned something. That is a genuine thing.

[PAUSE]

The instinct many people have at this point is to share it — with a partner, a friend, a sibling, an adult child who has been asking questions. Someone in your life who has heard you talking about this course or has been curious about AI and does not know where to start.

[PAUSE]

Sharing new knowledge is a good instinct. But how you share it matters.

[PAUSE]

Most people who try to teach technology to someone they love have had the experience of it going badly. Not because the technology is hard, and not because the other person is not capable. Because the way it was taught created pressure, or confusion, or a sense of being behind that made the whole thing feel like a problem rather than an opportunity.

[PAUSE]

This lesson is about how to bring someone else into AI without creating that experience.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "The three types of people to bring along"]

The people you might want to bring along tend to fall into three categories.

[PAUSE]

The first is someone who is curious but has not started. They have heard about AI, they have seen things about it, and they are interested but have not felt confident enough to try it themselves. This is the easiest person to help.

[PAUSE]

The second is someone who is sceptical. They have decided, or half-decided, that this is not for them — that it is too complicated, or not necessary, or for younger people, or something they will get around to eventually. This requires a different approach.

[PAUSE]

The third is someone who is anxious or resistant. They have seen frightening coverage. They are worried about safety, privacy, the effect on society. Trying to talk them into AI will create friction. A different kind of entry point is needed.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "For the curious person"]

For someone who is curious, the most effective approach is to show them something useful — not something impressive.

[PAUSE]

Impressive AI demonstrations tend to be abstract: "look at this, it can write a poem in the style of Shakespeare." The person is impressed and does not know what to do with that information in relation to their own life.

[PAUSE]

Useful demonstrations are specific to their situation.

"You mentioned you have been trying to write a letter to [organisation] about [problem]. Let me show you how I would start that."

"You said you have been reading that lease agreement and finding it confusing. Here is how I would ask AI to explain it."

"You were worried about the appointment next week. Let me show you how to put together a question list."

[PAUSE]

Something that connects to something real in their life. Something they can see themselves doing.

Then let them try it. Do not do it for them. Show it once, then hand over the keyboard.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "For the sceptical person"]

For someone who is sceptical, don't argue. Don't push.

[PAUSE]

The most effective thing you can do is let them observe you using AI naturally — not as a demonstration, but as part of how you handle tasks. "Just a moment, I'm going to ask AI about this." Then ask, and let them see the response.

[PAUSE]

If they have a question: "How does it know all that?" or "What do you do when it's wrong?" — answer it honestly and without selling. You are not trying to convert them. You are being available as a resource.

[PAUSE]

Sceptics often come around when they have a practical need and no obvious alternative. A document they cannot understand. A letter they need to write but are struggling with. A situation where they can see that the tool would genuinely help.

When that moment comes, if you have been available without pressure, they will often ask for help rather than having to be persuaded.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "For the anxious or resistant person"]

For someone who is anxious or resistant, the goal is not to persuade them to use AI. It is to help them feel less frightened by it.

[PAUSE]

The scam and safety content from Module 5 is often more useful here than the productivity content. Someone who is worried about being deceived or having their information stolen will respond better to "here is what the scam looks like and how to recognise it" than to "here is how you can draft letters faster."

Feeling informed is not the same as feeling enthusiastic. But it is a foundation.

[PAUSE]

Do not push this person to try AI before they are ready. The experience of being pressured into something that felt frightening and then struggling with it will reinforce resistance rather than dissolve it.

[PAUSE]

[ON SCREEN: "Practical ways to help someone get started"]

If someone is ready to start, here are the most useful things you can do.

[PAUSE]

Sit with them for the first session. Not to take over, but to be present when they get stuck. Stuck moments without anyone to ask are where new users give up. Stuck moments with someone beside them are where they learn.

[PAUSE]

Help them with the account setup. The process is not difficult, but for someone who has never done it, the questions about data privacy settings and opting out of training can feel like traps. Having someone who has already done it makes it feel safe.

[PAUSE]

Give them a prompt template. Not a blank screen. One specific, useful prompt that applies to something in their life. The complaint letter template. The document summary template. Something concrete they can try immediately.

[PAUSE]

Check in after a week. Not to quiz them, but to ask genuinely: "Did you try anything? What happened?" That follow-up says the thing matters and creates space for them to ask questions they might not have known who to ask.

[PAUSE]

The most important thing: your patience. The person you are helping is where you were at the start of this course. The discomfort you remember feeling, the uncertainty, the slight worry about getting it wrong — be the person who made that feel manageable, not the person who made them feel slow.

[PAUSE]

This is one of the most valuable things you can do with what you have learned. Not just using it yourself — passing it on.
