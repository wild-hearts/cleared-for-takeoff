## Where AI helps around care (safely)

AI is not a clinician, and it never will be. What it can do is take some of the writing and admin that piles up around care off your plate, so you get a calmer first draft and you stay in control of the rest. Here are the places it genuinely earns its keep, with patient information kept firmly out.

**Drafting referrals and patient letters.** You give it the structure and the clinical points in your own words, with no names, no dates of birth, no Medicare numbers and no health details, and it returns a tidy, professional template. You fill in the real specifics yourself, in your own system, and you read every word before it goes anywhere.

**Turning clinical shorthand into plain English.** Your dot points and abbreviations become a clear explanation a patient or family can actually follow. The meaning stays yours. You check it is accurate and gentle before anyone reads it.

**Drafting policies and procedures.** A privacy policy, an infection-control procedure, a complaints process. AI gives you a sensible first draft to react to instead of a blank page, which is often the slowest part.

**Wording recalls and reminders.** Getting the tone right on a recall or a reminder message, warm but clear, takes longer than it should. AI drafts a few versions and you pick and polish.

**Summarising a long guideline.** Paste in a published guideline or fact sheet, not patient records, and ask for the key points in plain language for your team or your waiting room.

**First drafts of newsletters and website copy.** Practice updates, a new-service announcement, an about page. A draft to edit, not a finished thing to trust.

Three rules hold all of this together, and they do not bend. Patient names, dates of birth, Medicare numbers and health details never go into AI. AI never makes a clinical decision. The practitioner checks everything before it leaves the room. This is the admin and communication around care, never the care itself.

## Common questions about AI in health and care

**Is it safe with patient information?**
Only if you keep patient information out of it. Used for non-identifying admin and communication, like a referral template or a policy draft, it is a useful tool. You decide what goes in, and you stay in control of the file.

**Can it replace clinical judgement?**
No, never. It drafts and it explains. The practitioner decides. Every clinical call, and every draft, is checked by a person before it counts.

**Where would I even start?**
Somewhere low-stakes. A policy draft, or a plain-English explainer for your waiting room, fully de-identified. You will see what it is good at, and where it needs your hand, without anything sensitive ever being involved.
