## Where AI gives a tiny team extra hands

Most community groups run on too few people and too little time. A coordinator who is also the bookkeeper, the social media person and the one who remembers the gate code. AI will not fix the funding or the roster, but it can take a real chunk of the desk work off your plate so more of your energy goes where it actually matters.

Here is where small organisations are genuinely getting value from it.

**Grant applications and acquittals.** Paste in the question, your notes and last year's numbers, and ask it to draft an answer that fits the word limit. It is good at structure and at tightening waffle into something a funder can read quickly. You bring the facts and the story. It saves you the staring-at-a-blank-box hours. Acquittals are the same: feed it your activity notes and let it shape the report.

**One update, many places.** Write your news once, then ask it to turn that into a member newsletter, a short Facebook post and a warm thank-you to a donor. Same information, three voices, ten minutes instead of an afternoon.

**Member and volunteer communications.** Rosters confirmations, reminder emails, the slightly awkward message asking someone to renew. It will draft the kind one for you so you only have to edit.

**Event planning.** Ask for a run sheet and a checklist for your fete, AGM or working bee, and it will give you a sensible starting list you can cut down. It thinks of the boring things you forget at 11pm.

**Board and funder reports.** Hand it your minutes or your figures and ask for a plain summary for the next meeting. You check every line, because the judgement is yours, but the first draft is done.

**Repurposing what you already wrote.** That grant story becomes an annual report paragraph, a speech, a poster blurb. Nothing wasted.

Be honest with yourself about what it is not. It does not know your members, it does not hold the relationships, and it does not make the mission calls. Treat it as a fast, tireless assistant who needs checking, not an expert. And the tools that do all this, ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, are free to use. For a group with no budget, that is the whole point.

## Common questions about AI for non-profits

**Can it really help with grant applications?**
Yes, for drafting, structuring and tightening, which is where most of the time goes. You give it the real numbers and the heart of why your work matters, and it shapes that into clear answers that fit the questions. What it cannot do is know the truth of your organisation, so you check every claim, every figure and every promise before it goes anywhere near a funder. It writes the draft. You stay accountable for it.

**We have no budget. Is this for us?**
Especially you. The free versions of the main tools are genuinely enough for everything above, and you do not need to pay for anything to get started. That is the point of teaching this to community groups. The organisations with the least money to spend on staff time are the ones who get the most back from a tool that hands them a few hours a week.

**Is it hard for volunteers to pick up?**
No. There is no software to learn and no jargon. You type what you want in plain words, the way you would ask a capable helper, and it answers. A volunteer who can write an email can do this on day one. The handy part is that the skill carries across the whole team, so once one person gets the hang of it they can show the next, and you are not stuck depending on a single tech-savvy member.
